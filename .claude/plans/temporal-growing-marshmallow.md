# Analysis: Course Literature Papers vs. Theoretical Framework

## Context

The user asked for a thorough review of ALL papers in both Course Literature Lists (Transformative Game Design 2; Cultivating Transformational Communities) to identify which theoretical frameworks would constitute truly relevant additions to the theoretical framework chapter.

- **Transformational container** (Bowman 2021, Winnicott 1960, Huizinga 1958, Deterding 2017)
- **Group dynamics** (Tuckman 1965, Yalom 2020)

- **Designable surfaces** (Bowman et al. 2025)

Sources already cited total ~25+. The framework is dense and substantive. Any addition must fill a genuine gap, not merely add another voice to an already-established point.

## Tier 1: High Relevance — Fill a Genuine Theoretical Gap

### 1. Toft & Harrer (2020) — "Design Bleed: A Standpoint Methodology for Game Design"
- **Source:** DiGRA 2020 proceedings (peer-reviewed)
- **Appears in:** Cultivating Transformational Communities, Required Readings
- **Concept:** "Design bleed" — how designers' identities, values, and standpoints bleed into the game systems they create, shaping participant experience in ways the designer may not intend
- **Where it fits:** Value Convergence / Evaluative Bleed-in section (line ~120)
- **Why it's relevant:** The thesis coins "evaluative bleed-in" for judges. Toft & Harrer provide a *published, peer-reviewed* theoretical framework for the closely related process of authority-figure standpoint bleeding into game structure. "Design bleed" is not about player-character bleed — it's specifically about the designer/facilitator's standpoint shaping the system. This is almost exactly evaluative bleed-in from the design side. Currently, evaluative bleed-in is supported only by the Bastarrachea case. Toft & Harrer provide the *theoretical framework* for why and how it happens.
- **Gap filled:** Theoretical grounding for evaluative bleed-in as a systemic process (not just an individual authority-figure phenomenon)

### 2. Hammer, Beltrán, Walton & Turkington (2018) — "Power and Control in Role-playing Games"
- **Source:** Routledge Handbook of Role-Playing Game Studies, pp. 448-467 (peer-reviewed handbook chapter)
- **Appears in:** TGD2, Recommended Readings
- **Concept:** Systematic theoretical framework for how power structures operate within RPG communities — who has power, how it's exercised, how it shapes play experience
- **Where it fits:** Competitive Pressure and the Compromised Container section (line ~132-138)
- **Why it's relevant:** The framework discusses competitive pressure and hierarchies using Bowman 2013 (social conflict), but Bowman 2013 is an *empirical/qualitative* study, not a theoretical framework for power. Hammer et al. provide a *systematic theoretical analysis* of power and control mechanisms in RPGs — covering formal authority (judges/GMs), social capital, gatekeeping, and how power shapes which transformations are possible. This is exactly what the competitive pressure section needs: not just documentation that hierarchies exist, but a theory of how power structures channel or block transformative potential.
- **Gap filled:** Theoretical lens for power dynamics (the framework currently describes the *symptom* — competitive pressure — without a *theory of power*)

### 3. Bowman (2018) — "Immersion and Shared Imagination in Role-Playing Games"
- **Source:** Routledge Handbook (Role-Playing Game Studies: Transmedia Foundations), pp. 379-394 (peer-reviewed)
- **Appears in:** TGD2, Required Readings
- **Concept:** A theoretical model of immersion as it operates in RPGs — distinguishing types of immersion, explaining how shared imagined spaces are co-constructed, and connecting immersion to identity and emotional experience
- **Where it fits:** Identity section (line ~15-23), between identity work and bleed
- **Why it's relevant:** The framework currently jumps from identity work (constructing a fragmented self) directly to bleed (spillover effects). What's missing is the *mechanism of absorption*: why does performing a debate position become so cognitively consuming that bleed occurs? Grounded cognition (Lankoski & Jarvelä) explains the *neurological* side; immersion theory explains the *experiential* side — the subjective state in which debaters become absorbed in their positions during intense rounds. Many debaters report "losing themselves" in argument; immersion theory names and explains this state. Moreover, Bowman's concept of "shared imagination" maps onto how debate teams co-construct their argumentative reality during preparation.
- **Gap filled:** The experiential mechanism connecting identity work to bleed (the framework explains *what* happens but not *how it feels* to be inside it)

## Tier 2: Moderate-High Relevance — Strengthens an Existing Section with RPG-Specific Theory

### 4. Leonard & Arango (2016) — "Conflict and Change: Testing a Life-cycle Derived Model of Larp Group Dynamics"
- **Source:** IJRP 7, pp. 15-22 (peer-reviewed)
- **Appears in:** Cultivating Transformational Communities, Required + Recommended Readings
- **Concept:** RPG-specific empirical model of group dynamics — how larp communities form, develop, experience conflict, and either evolve or dissolve
- **Where it fits:** Container section, group dynamics paragraph (line ~13)
- **Why it's relevant:** The group dynamics paragraph currently relies on *general* psychology (Tuckman 1965 — small group development) and *general* psychotherapy (Yalom 2020 — therapeutic group factors). These are well-established but not RPG-specific. Leonard & Arango provide the *only peer-reviewed, RPG-specific* group dynamics model in the literature. Their lifecycle model, empirically tested against larp communities, can be mapped onto debate community development in ways Tuckman's generic model cannot. Strengthens the claim that RPG theory specifically (not just general psychology) illuminates debate communities.
- **Gap filled:** RPG-specific group dynamics theory (currently reliant on general psychology)

### 5. Haarman (2022) — "Dungeons & Dragons & Dewey: The Potential for Dramatic Rehearsal and Civic Outcomes in Tabletop Role-Playing Games"
- **Source:** Philosophical Studies in Education 53, pp. 56-70 (peer-reviewed)
- **Appears in:** Cultivating Transformational Communities, Recommended Readings
- **Concept:** John Dewey's concept of "dramatic rehearsal" — mentally trying out courses of action and their consequences before committing — applied to RPGs as vehicles for democratic/civic education
- **Where it fits:** ZPD / Scaffolded Learning section (line ~61-63), or the Implications section
- **Why it's relevant:** The framework discusses debate's democratic outcomes (Hicks's "democratic ethos") and learning theory (Vygotsky, Kolb) but doesn't have a philosophical theory connecting role-playing specifically to democratic education. Dewey's "dramatic rehearsal" maps perfectly onto what switch-side debaters do: they rehearse arguing positions they don't hold, experiencing the consequences of different stances, and thereby develop civic capacity. Haarman's contribution is connecting this philosophical concept specifically to RPGs. This bridges the gap between the learning theory section and the debate-as-democratic-education claim.
- **Gap filled:** Philosophical theory connecting role-playing to democratic education (currently asserted via Hicks but not philosophically grounded)

## Tier 3: Moderate Relevance — Could Add Value but Not Essential

### 6. Bowman & Torner (2014) — "Post-Larp Depression"
- **Source:** Analog Game Studies 1(1) (not peer-reviewed in the traditional sense)
- **Concept:** Emotional aftermath of intense role-playing when integration/debrief is inadequate — participants experience depression, loss of meaning, difficulty returning to ordinary life
- **Where it fits:** Debrief section (line ~37-43)
- **Why it matters:** The debrief section argues that debate's oral critique is inadequate, but doesn't name what happens when debrief fails. Post-larp depression provides this vocabulary and demonstrates the stakes of inadequate debrief design.
- **Why it's Tier 3:** The concept is well-known in the field but the source is a blog-style publication, and the debrief section is already quite strong.

### 7. Koulu (2020) — "Emotions as Skilled Work"
- **Source:** What Do We Do When We Play? (Solmukohta anthology, not peer-reviewed)
- **Concept:** Emotional engagement in play as a *skill* that can be developed and practiced, not just something that happens to participants
- **Where it fits:** Bleed section or ZPD section
- **Why it matters:** Reframes the bleed discussion — experienced debaters don't just *undergo* bleed, they learn to *manage* it. This connects to the ZPD framework: the skill of managing bleed (navigating cognitive dissonance, maintaining strategic awareness while emotionally engaged) is itself scaffolded through coaching and experience.
- **Why it's Tier 3:** Solmukohta anthology (not peer-reviewed), and the concept, while interesting, doesn't fill a structural gap.

### 8. Pöllänen & Arjoranta (2021) — "'Whose Were Those Feelings?' Affect and Likenessing in Halat Hisar"
- **Source:** International Journal of Cultural Studies 24(6), pp. 899-916 (peer-reviewed)
- **Concept:** "Likenessing" — the process by which players sort out which feelings and thoughts from play belong to them versus their character. Affect theory applied to larp.
- **Where it fits:** Bleed section, after discussing bleed types (line ~27-35)
- **Why it matters:** Likenessing describes exactly what debaters do after rounds — sorting out which arguments they find genuinely persuasive versus which were performed strategically. This post-play processing is the cognitive work that either enables or prevents bleed from integrating.
- **Why it's Tier 3:** Published in a cultural studies journal (good for credibility), but the bleed section is already rich and adding another concept risks bloating it.

### 9. Geneuss (2021) — "The Use of the Role-playing Technique STARS in Formal Didactic Contexts"
- **Source:** IJRP 11, pp. 114-131 (peer-reviewed)
- **Concept:** STARS is a structured educational RPG technique designed for formal classroom use — bridges the gap between freeform RPG and structured curriculum
- **Where it fits:** ZPD / Educational RPG section (line ~61-63)
- **Why it matters:** Provides a concrete precedent for designing structured educational role-playing experiences within formal institutional settings — directly parallel to what the thesis is attempting for debate.
- **Why it's Tier 3:** More of a design precedent than a theoretical framework.

## Recommendation

**My recommendation is to add the 3 Tier 1 sources.** They each fill a genuine structural gap in the framework:

1. **Toft & Harrer (2020)** — grounds evaluative bleed-in in published theory
2. **Hammer et al. (2018)** — provides power theory for the competitive pressure section
3. **Bowman (2018)** — provides the immersion mechanism connecting identity to bleed

The Tier 2 sources (Leonard & Arango 2016; Haarman 2022) are strong candidates if you want broader diversification. Leonard & Arango is particularly appealing because it's IJRP peer-reviewed and RPG-specific. Haarman connects to democratic education in a way that strengthens the thesis's civic claims.

The Tier 3 sources are genuinely interesting but risk bloating an already dense chapter. I would not recommend adding them unless you feel a specific section is weak.
