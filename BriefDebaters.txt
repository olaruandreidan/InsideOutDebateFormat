Rolling in:

- the Subject team has to choose a name, and other small personal details that they present

Rolling out: 
- the Subject thanks the Value Defenders for speaking their truths 
- the Value Defenders thank the Subject for listening
- then they all say who they are and shake hands with everyone else, thanking them for playing together "Andrei. Thank you for playing with me"

Instructions for the Value Defenders:
- formulate in your prep time arguments for why the value you are defending is important, is useful, and is especially relevant to the dilemma
- formulate a particular decision to the dilemma, and argue why it is the one that is most in-tune with your value: why it is right and why it would lead to good outcomes, especially when analysed from the lens of your value
- in response to the speeches of the other Value Defenders, engage both with the value that they are defending (explaining why it might be subordinate to your value, or less relevant to the dilemma, or down-right dangerous and should be ditched) and with the decision they propose.
- in response to the speeches of the other Value Defenders, choose wisely how you position visavi the decision they propose. You can choose to attack it, to support it (claiming however that its main strength is in the way in which it maximises the value that you are defending) or to support it with addendums. Remember, your main job is to define for the Subject what it would mean to respect the value you are defending, and to prove that it is the right thing to do, you are not prescribed a particular decision to support. 

Instructions for the Subject: 
- try to embody the Subject of the motion when listening to the debate. Engage with the speeches of the Value Defenders as with those of close friends, or voices in your head. 
- do not enter the debate with an opinion. Listen to it as someone that is completely and utterly torn and has no idea what best to do, that decides to just listen to the points made by the Value Defenders and then to choose one of the decisions that they championed based on their earnest evaluation of the interplay between all arguments brought forth and the degree to which they were persuasively presented
- when listening to arguments and awaiting to be persuaded, try to first of all just embody the Subject as you imagine them. Listen as if you are them, let yourself be moved by the speeches of the Defenders as if you are them. After you listen to an argument take also a mental step back and also intellectually consider if it was presented in a manner that would have been persuasive for the Subject, and adjust your opinion accordingly 
- try to take notes of the arguments and of their interplay. Do not add however personal thoughts about this, focus on what was said by the Value Defenders, as if by voices in your head. Do not add another voice to that chorus. 
- discuss quietly with your partner, to make sure you collaborate towards reaching the best decision, the one that makes most sense of the Subject given what was said and that takes in consideration everything to the extent to which it was persuasive. Make sure to not be in conflict to your partner: in the end, you are both embodying the same Subject. 
- in your speeches present towards what decision you are leaning, and why. When arguing why, try to take into consideration as much as possible of what was said, and to explain the extent to which they were persuasive and the interplay between all arguments brought forth

Instructions for the Evaluator: 
- analyze the debate and the performance of each player. Look at the commendations that you have available, and think if any of them would be appropriate to award
(just for this debate: try to award one commendation to each debater. You can award the same commendation to more debaters)
- look at the debate from the perspective of an educator that tries to help the players grow
- storytell the debate from your perspective, from the outside. Say what each team did and how it ended
- engage directly afterwards with each team. First of all ask them what they believe they did well, and what they didn’t do so well or had a hard time doing. Engage with what they said and explain your opinion, both on the points they raised and also on other points you viewed as important. Ask them what they would like to do better next debate, and give them advice how to achieve that. 
- award each of them at the end the commendations you chose
