\section{Conclusion}

Alfred ``Tuna'' Snider was first to recognize debate as a game and to apply play logic to its analysis, doing this way before game design was a recognized discipline. Through his career as a teacher and scholar he championed the transformative power of playing this game with joy, passion and determination. Although many of his students, one of whom I was very privileged to be, did continue his work in fields such as civics, education, rhetoric and his own specialty of forensics, none used game-design know-how to answer the question that most naturally flows from his argument that debate is a game: how can we make it a better game? This thesis takes one step into that question.

A ``how can'' research question unfolds in three stages, and it is worth being explicit about which of them this thesis answered. The first stage asks whether the task is approachable at all---whether the conceptual tools required even apply to the object of study. Sections \ref{sec:background} and \ref{sec:theory} answer this: debate is a class of transformative role-playing games, and the apparatus of transformative game design can be brought to bear on it without conceptual distortion. The second stage asks whether a concrete attempt at the task can be carried through. Sections \ref{sec:methods}--\ref{sec:design} answer this by documenting the design of the Inside Out format and the iterative process through which it arrived at its current shape. The third stage asks whether the attempt produced anything of value. The results and discussion chapters answer this in their respective registers: clue-by-clue proof that the format behaves as theorized, and theory-facing extensions opened up by the encounter between framework and playtest.

What the thesis does \emph{not} claim is that the Inside Out format, in its current state, solves the problems identified in the problem statement. It takes a first and not strictly necessary step in a much longer process of reform, whose later stages involve refinement, community adoption, governance design, and the slow work of shifting a meta. Making a game is the smaller problem; carrying one into a community with established governance, calcified metas, and invested practitioners is the larger one, and this thesis does not attempt it.

The enduring contribution of this thesis is best stated as a single claim, of which the work's more specific outputs are each an instance. In a transformative competitive format, the rulebook is the smallest of the surfaces that shape the participant's experience. The real work of design sits in the apparatus that surrounds it: the roles participants inhabit, the stances they take toward those roles, the agencies the rules authorize, the peer economy that awards recognition, the evaluative practices that witness growth, and the governance structure that decides, over time, what the rulebook is taken to mean. Transformative game design theory has been built in contexts where that apparatus is thin---LARP weekends, indie tabletop sessions, play spaces where the designer's specification reaches the participant with minimal mediation. Competitive debate has a dense apparatus, and what this thesis offers is the beginning of a vocabulary for designing it.

That vocabulary appears in three registers. First, seven theoretical extensions, each locating a piece of the apparatus: genre alibi as the filter that decides which framings the container will accept; the judge-as-average-person as the mechanism that drives value convergence regardless of individual judges' backgrounds; steering as the construct that lets a player hold a role both from inside and from outside; Nguyen's agency-shaping as the altitude at which Bogost's procedural rhetoric becomes most clearly visible; social recognition as a third source of transformational pressure alongside competition and progression; growth legibility as the designable surface without which the debrief cannot reinforce what the play produces; and governance structure as the layer that decides, over time, what any ruleset is taken to mean. Second, five modular components of Inside Out---the commendation system, the split between an in-debate Interlocutor and an external Evaluator, three teams, a judge who roleplays a specific named character, and value-prescribed teams with solution freedom---each transplantable into existing formats without rewriting their rulebooks. This transplantability is itself a direct expression of the overarching claim: if the apparatus carries the transformation, then pieces of apparatus can be grafted onto existing rulesets and produce transformative effects on their own terms. Third, the demonstration that the intersection of game design scholarship and forensics pedagogy is productive terrain, precisely because competitive debate's institutional density makes visible an apparatus that softer play contexts never force the designer to see. The thesis's most durable invitation is therefore not to adopt Inside Out, but to treat the apparatus surrounding a competitive format as the real designable object, and to bring game designers into the work of shaping it.

What this thesis carries forward, then, is not a finished format but three things that are arguably more durable: a set of insights about transformative debate formats that outlast any one design; my emotional momentum required to pursue the subsequent stages of reform that the design itself cannot deliver; and evidence, however preliminary, that releasing game designers into the problem space of debate is a move worth making at the scale of the field. Snider's lineage held the insight that debate is a game played for transformation; the work he pointed to without undertaking was the work of designing debate as one would design any other game. Opening that work to game designers---and inviting the debate community to take seriously what they produce---is the lasting invitation this thesis hopes to extend.
