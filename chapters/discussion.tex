\section{Discussion}

The results chapter established, clue by clue, that Inside Out behaves the way a transformative debate format is supposed to behave. This discussion does something different: it reads the playtests back through the theoretical framework of Section~\ref{sec:theory} to ask where the theory explains what happened, where the results press on its edges, and where the encounter between the two points toward extensions the framework needs if it is to operate at the level of precision intentional design demands. Several of the most striking findings emerged not because the framework correctly predicted them but in spite of its silence on them; those silences are the productive output of this thesis.

The extensions that follow address seemingly separate surfaces---alibi, judges, players, agencies, peers, evaluators, governance---but they converge on a single claim, and it is worth stating it here so the reader can read each subsection as evidence for it. \textit{In a transformative competitive format, the rulebook is the smallest of the surfaces that shape the participant's experience.} The real work of design sits in the apparatus that surrounds the rulebook: the roles participants inhabit, the stances they take toward those roles, the agencies the rules authorize, the peer economy that awards recognition, the evaluative practices that witness growth, and the governance structure that decides, over time, what the rulebook is taken to mean. Transformative game design theory has been formulated largely in contexts where this apparatus is thin---LARP weekends, indie tabletop sessions, play spaces where the designer's specification reaches the participant with minimal mediation. Competitive debate has a dense institutional apparatus, and each extension below is the shape the theory takes when it has to cross it.

\subsection{Genre Alibi and the Filter on What Survives in a Format's Meta}

\textcite{deterding2017alibis}'s account of alibi treats it as a social license to participate: ``I was assigned this side'' deflects inference from the player's displayed behavior to the game's structure. The playtests show the concept needs a second axis. The Rolling Out ritual---mechanically identical in intent to any framing device used in transformative LARP---was reported as ``very robotic'' in the first playtest and drew mumbling in the second, while the term ``Inner Voices'' was adopted spontaneously by the same participants. Both moves are licensed by the alibi. What distinguished them was whether they matched the genre participants had licensed themselves to be there for. Debate's social contract treats ceremonial language as frivolous; roleplay-adjacent self-naming, by contrast, reads as imaginative investment and is kept. The alibi grants permission; the genre of the alibi filters which permissions are cashed in.

This has a direct implication for the framework. \textcite{bowman2025transformative}'s list of designable surfaces treats the container's framing and ritual structure as one such surface, but does not distinguish between alibi-as-license and alibi-as-genre-declaration. A framing device appropriate to a LARP weekend can be procedurally correct in a debate format and still fail, because the genre of the format sets a prior over what participants will accept. Future research should examine systematically how the declared purpose of an alibi---fun, transformation, professional formation---shapes which ritual forms survive contact with a given community.

\subsection{Value Convergence Revisited: The Judge as the Average Unbiased Person}

The framework reframes value convergence as corrupted memetic bleed: judges sharing common backgrounds produce evaluative bleed-in that teaches debaters the community's strategic equilibrium rather than epistemic diversity (Section~\ref{sec:theory}). The playtests support this mechanism but also sharpen it. The underlying pathology is not simply that judges happen to be demographically similar; it is that they are all asked to roleplay the \textit{same} archetype---``the average, unbiased person''---and the words ``average'' and ``unbiased'' are themselves ideologically loaded. Everyone converging on one role produces drift regardless of the judges' individual backgrounds, because the role itself pulls interpretation toward a single imagined audience and sanctions deviation. Inside Out's second-playtest debriefs captured this almost verbatim: participants contrasted the S0 speech, which frames bias as perspective rather than contamination, against ``the pretense of objectivity'' they located in standard formats, and argued that the standard framing itself propagates the notion that only one kind of ``objective'' person can judge well.

Four structural choices in Inside Out act on this mechanism simultaneously: the Interlocutor roleplays a specific named character rather than a composite neutral; there are three teams rather than two; persuasion targets values rather than policy; and the S0 speech grants the Subject worldbuilding license, providing an explicit alibi to interpret the same character differently across rounds. Switch-side debating already cultivates humility through its own procedural rhetoric \parencite{bogost2007persuasive}; the design task is to defend that humility against the homogenizing pressure that otherwise leaks back in. The framework extension this suggests is that the judge-role's psychological specificity is a first-order design variable, not a community-support afterthought. Further research should compare circuits differing in judge-role specification---named-character, demographically-scripted, explicitly partisan---to map how judge-role pluralism shapes the memetic pathways available to debaters.

\subsection{Steering: A Missing Construct for the Subject's Dual Stance}

The first playtest's Subject described not knowing ``what to say,'' caught between emotional investment and evaluative neutrality; the second playtest's Subject resolved the tension by leaning into the roleplay rather than maintaining analytical detachment. The framework, as written, has no construct that names what the second Subject did. Bleed describes what flows into the player; alibi describes the social license to let it in; neither captures the player's capacity to inhabit the character while retaining an external stance to notice what the scene is doing.

The Nordic theory of steering \parencite{montola2015steering,pohjola2015steering} is precisely this missing construct. Steering describes the player's ongoing negotiation between in-character inhabitation and out-of-character authorship---the capacity to play ``as if from inside'' while still shaping the fiction from outside. The transformative game design literature has absorbed bleed almost without qualification but has treated steering as a LARP-practitioner term rather than a theoretical primitive, and the omission becomes costly the moment a format asks a participant to be both a roleplayer and a reflective judge of the roleplay. Importing steering into the framework does two things: it names what the successful Subject did, and it makes the Subject's evaluative burden a design parameter (how much steering the role requires) rather than an individual skill problem.

\subsection{Procedural Rhetoric and Agency: Nguyen Extends Bogost}

Several of the strongest results were procedural rhetoric effects in \textcite{bogost2007persuasive}'s sense: the emergent norm against adversarial rebuttal, the collaborative drift toward synthesis in the second round of Inner Voice speeches, and the spontaneous roleplay stance all arose from the format's structural logic rather than from instruction. \textcite{nguyen2020games}'s account of games as the art of agency restates this point at a different altitude, and its addition to the framework is productive. Games shape players not only by the worldviews their rules encode (Bogost) but by the agencies they make available (Nguyen): what kinds of action, what kinds of deliberation, what shape of success the format authorizes the player to pursue. Inside Out authorizes a different agency than traditional debate---collaborative persuasion oriented toward a Subject's decision rather than zero-sum victory over an opponent---and the emergent norms are what that different authorization looks like in play.

The framework extension is modest but useful: procedural rhetoric describes the argument a format makes; agency-shaping describes the kind of player it cultivates. Transformation sits at the intersection, and both lenses are needed to design for it.

\subsection{Social Recognition as the Pressure Mechanism}

The framework anticipated that removing win-loss outcomes would require a replacement source of competitive motivation, and the commendation architecture was designed to provide it. The playtests report something the framework did not predict: the mechanic driving high effort was neither the win/loss dynamic nor primarily the commendation system, but a social one---participants wanted to be recognized and appreciated by their fellow Inner Voices and by the Subject for the quality and resonance of their contributions. This is proven both by their desire to do polite counter-argumentation and by the reporting of a desire of one participant to just have their point understood. Pressure was present and productive. Its source was peer regard, not progression.

This is a genuine gap in the existing transformative game design literature, which has extensive theory about the container, alibi, and bleed, but little about what sustains motivational intensity in the container's absence of external competition. Social recognition within a bounded group is itself a transformational pressure: it aligns with the format's goals (collaborative persuasion is what gets recognized), it operates at the speed of the session (no need for circuit-scale memory), and it is durable under the removal of win-loss. The framework extension is to name this explicitly as a third category of pressure alongside competitive stakes and progression architecture, and to treat it as a designable surface in its own right. Future research should examine the conditions under which social recognition remains aligned with transformative goals rather than degrading into in-group status-seeking.

\subsection{Growth Legibility: The Undertheorized Designable Surface}

The commendation system succeeded in decoupling status from competition and was received with genuine enthusiasm (``scratching the make-a-build itch''), but evaluators reported difficulty assigning commendations consistently, and one raised the risk of ``farming''---stylized play optimized for commendation coverage rather than development. Both difficulties point to the same problem: the framework specifies what transformation \textit{is} (bleed, identity work, memetic uptake) but not how a facilitator \textit{sees} it occurring in real time, and not how a progression architecture can be designed so that optimizing for its rewards equals the development it is meant to measure.

\textit{Growth legibility} names this missing surface. It has two components. \textit{Observational legibility} requires behavioral specification of what growth looks like---not ``epistemic humility'' as an aspiration but something a trained evaluator can mark as it happens in a five-minute speech---together with evaluators trained to distinguish genuine transformation from its performance. \textit{Anti-farming legibility} requires that commendation criteria be tight enough that optimizing for them \textit{is} developing the underlying capacity; underspecified criteria produce hollow compliance and actively interfere with transformation by rewarding its simulation. The reason the framework has not theorized this is that most transformative LARP and RPG research does not include formalized evaluation: participants self-assess, peers offer informal feedback, and the question of what growth looks like to an evaluator never arises. Competitive debate makes the gap impossible to avoid, and in doing so makes it designable. Growth legibility belongs on \textcite{bowman2025transformative}'s list of designable surfaces.

\subsection{Governance, Not Rules, Carries the Meta}

The second playtest debrief produced the most important empirical observation in this thesis, and it is about something the framework does not address at all. Participants reconstructed how the drift toward speedtalking in British Parliamentary, and its later contamination of World Schools after the judge pools merged, was a \textit{governance} story rather than a rules story. As the founding university teachers disengaged from BP, rule interpretation fell to whoever held social power in the circuit, and they systematically privileged the skill hardest to master---logical density---because that was what sustained their standing. A new format dropped into that community inherits the same governance pathology regardless of its ruleset.

The framework treats community support as a condition on the container's function, following \textcite{bowman2025transformative}. The playtests suggest community support is insufficient as a category: what matters is the \textit{governance layer}---how rules are interpreted, how evaluators are trained, how motions are authored, how commendations are constructed and displayed across a season, and whose voice sets the meta's direction of drift. This layer is not designed in this thesis, and it is more consequential for the format's long-run behavior than anything in the format specification. The framework extension is to recognize governance structure as a designable surface of its own, outside the framing of a single game, sitting above the format rather than alongside it. Comparative research on circuits where formal rules are stable but governance structures differ (pre- and post-merger WS is a natural case) would test whether governance alone predicts meta drift in the way the playtest debrief suggests it does.

\subsection{Modularity, and an Honest Note on the Process}

The framework tends to treat transformative formats as integrated wholes whose components reinforce one another. Inside Out's design is also that, but it is additionally decomposable into five components each of which could be transplanted into an existing format without the others: the commendation system; splitting the judge into an in-debate Interlocutor whose performance is itself evaluated and an external Evaluator whose is not; three teams rather than two; a judge who roleplays a specific named character; and teams prescribed values but granted creative freedom over the solutions that express them. The framework extension is a modularity lens: treating transformative designs as candidate sets of transplantable mechanisms rather than monolithic experiences.

Two honest notes complete this discussion. First, the thesis narrates a deductive arc from theory to design, but the design emerged intuitively through many conversations with practitioners and was retrofitted to theoretical justification afterwards. That the retrofitting held testifies to the coherence of the theoretical framework, not to the process by which this particular design was reached; readers should not take the chapter order as a claim about design epistemology. Second, the format's audience is structurally narrow. Only teenagers and students in their early twenties have the time budget sustained deliberative play demands, which is itself the reason playtest recruitment was difficult and which bounds the populations to whom these findings can be expected to generalize. Both notes are limitations of the work and, equally, parts of the theoretical contribution: they indicate that transformative game design applied to existing competitive communities is not a theory-first engineering exercise, and that the framework's predictive reach is bounded by audiences whose life structure permits the kind of sustained engagement the theory assumes.
