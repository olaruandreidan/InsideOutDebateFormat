\section{RESULTS AND DISCUSSION}
\label{sec:results}

This chapter addresses the central research question:

\begin{quote}
How can the application of transformative game design theory inform the creation of a competitive debate format that manages to elevate its transformative potential---improved critical thinking, discourse quality, and self-empowerment---by addressing the issues of value convergence, discourse speed, and community hierarchy?
\end{quote}

The question admits two decomposed sub-questions. First: can a method of applying transformative game design theory to this problem domain be demonstrated? Second: does that method yield expected results? Each is addressed in turn.

\subsection{Demonstrating the Method: The Design Process as Contribution}

The first sub-question is answered by the thesis itself. The preceding chapters document a systematic progression from theoretical analysis (Sections~\ref{sec:background} and~\ref{sec:theory}), through iterative format design grounded in design theory principles (Section~\ref{sec:design}), to empirical testing and reflection. This sequence constitutes a worked example of how transformative game design theory can be operationalized in a competitive debate context. The design decisions recorded in Annexes A, B, and G---and the reasoning connecting each iteration to observed player behavior---demonstrate that such an application is coherent, achievable, and produces testable outcomes. Whether or not the resulting format succeeds transformatively, the method of application has been rendered explicit and repeatable.

\subsection{Evaluating the Results: Evidence from Playtests}

The second sub-question concerns whether the designed format shows signs of achieving its intended effects. Two playtests were conducted: the first in March 2026 with senior debaters from the Jean Monet debate club in Bucharest, and the second in April 2026 with seven veteran debaters in an informal setting. Field notes, structured debrief responses (Annex C), and post-session reflections constitute the primary data.

It is important to frame what this data can and cannot show. Given the small sample size, the absence of control conditions, and the exploratory nature of the design, the collected data cannot prove the format's effectiveness. What it can provide are \textit{clues}---observed behaviors, reported experiences, and emergent patterns---from which educated inferences about potential effectiveness may be drawn. The following analysis examines clues across five evaluation fields. A full enumeration of identified clues, with explanations of what each points toward and why, is provided in Annex~H.

\subsubsection{Field 1: Engagement, Appeal, and Long-Term Viability}

For a debate format to sustain a community, it must be sufficiently engaging to generate voluntary, repeated participation. Several clues suggest the Inside Out format clears this threshold.

Players across both playtests spontaneously expressed that the format was enjoyable. In the first playtest, participants described it as ``surprisingly liberating'' and reported ``a lot less anxiety'' than in standard competitive rounds. In the second playtest, the Huddle phase was described by multiple participants as ``very very fun,'' and players indicated they would choose to play again, noting they would try to be ``more emotionally immersed'' on a second encounter. The organic adoption of format vocabulary---participants spontaneously referring to each other as ``inner voices'' during the second playtest, a term not present in the ruleset---suggests genuine imaginative investment.

A significant finding is the differential appeal across participant profiles. Women and debaters who had been less successful in traditional formats expressed strong preference for Inside Out. This suggests the format reaches constituencies underserved by standard competitive debate, potentially expanding the participant base rather than merely offering an alternative experience to existing enthusiasts. The one participant who found the format unsatisfying was a highly competitive debater who missed the ``ego-boosting'' feeling of decisive victory---a response consistent with the format's design intent to deprioritize individual dominance.

\subsubsection{Field 2: Productive Pressure Mechanisms}

Competitive debate's transformative potential depends partly on pressure: the cognitive and social demands of the activity push participants to grow. A format without sufficient challenge risks becoming a low-stakes social exercise without developmental benefit. Clues from both playtests suggest Inside Out retains meaningful pressure while redistributing its character.

Participants in the first playtest noted that ``architecting solutions'' was ``very complex and interesting strategically.'' Rather than reducing the cognitive challenge, the format redirected it: instead of constructing chains of consequences or maximizing argumentative coverage, players found themselves navigating value hierarchies, contextual reasoning, and audience empathy. This represents a shift in the \textit{kind} of intellectual pressure rather than a reduction in degree.

The Commendations system (Annex A) provides a recognition mechanism that acknowledges growth without binary win/loss outcomes. While facilitators in both playtests noted that commendations were challenging to assign without clear performance targets, participants who received them reported the recognition as meaningful. One evaluator observed that in a larger circuit, commendations would become more organic---pointing toward a format whose evaluative culture could mature with practice.

\subsubsection{Field 3: Growth Direction---Useful Skills and Perspectives}

A central design goal was to redirect the skills debate develops toward more transferable outcomes: persuasive communication, perspective-taking, contextual reasoning, and collaborative discourse. Clues from both playtests suggest this redirection was partially achieved.

In the first playtest, participants articulated learning to ``think about the context of decisions,'' to be ``more human and more honest about human nature,'' and to recognize that ``sometimes decisions are personal, not universal.'' These reflections align with the format's procedural rhetoric \parencite{bogost2007persuasive}: the rules of Inside Out encode a world in which personal context and value conflict are central to decision-making, and players appear to have internalized aspects of this world-view through play.

The second playtest produced particularly notable evidence of perspective-taking. All players immediately adopted a roleplay-oriented stance, speaking directly to the Subject, using second-person address, and adding contextualizing details to the motion. This spontaneous behavior---not explicitly instructed in the ruleset---suggests that the format's structure reliably elicits the kind of imaginative engagement associated with perspective-taking bleed \parencite{hugaas2024bleed}. The Subject player's reflection on how the opening speech led them to ``expose some nuanced biases''---a bleed of player into character, and character reasoning back into player---represents a concrete instance of the memetic bleed mechanism theorized in Section~\ref{sec:theory}.

\subsubsection{Field 4: Community Dynamics and Reduced Toxicity}

One of the design's primary goals was to reduce the hierarchical and adversarial dynamics that contribute to attrition and harm in competitive debate communities. Several clues suggest the format meaningfully shifts these dynamics.

The most striking finding was the cultural norm that emerged spontaneously during the second playtest: participants noted that ``outright attacking others' points feels wrong, disrespectful, and unnecessary.'' This was not a rule imposed by the format but a norm that emerged from the format's structure---a textbook example of procedural rhetoric reshaping participant behavior \parencite{bogost2007persuasive}. Players found that shifting focus and building on others' contributions felt ``more natural and more persuasive'' than traditional rebuttal, suggesting a genuine rhetorical culture shift.

Both playtests confirmed significantly reduced anxiety. Participants across both sessions described the experience as ``much much less anxiety-inducing'' than standard competitive debate, while still finding it stimulating and structured enough to sustain engagement. One participant offered an important nuance: the reduced anxiety may partly reflect the format's lesser established status, so that less social capital is staked on performance. This is an honest limitation to note---whether anxiety reduction would persist in an established Inside Out circuit is an empirical question for future study.

\subsubsection{Field 5: Epistemic Humility}

The deepest transformative aim of the Inside Out format is to cultivate epistemic humility: a disposition to take multiple perspectives seriously, to recognize the validity of different value frameworks, and to hold one's own conclusions with appropriate uncertainty. Clues from both playtests point toward the format supporting this outcome.

In the first playtest, participants explicitly articulated a version of epistemic humility: ``every team says valuable stuff and it is important more to see what is important to us.'' This recognition---that competing perspectives each contain genuine insight---is the precise attitudinal outcome theorized in the bleed and procedural rhetoric frameworks of Section~\ref{sec:theory}.

The second playtest produced two particularly notable pieces of evidence. First, the spontaneous drift toward consensus in the second round of Value Defender speeches: rather than competing to ``win,'' players naturally moved toward finding the most defensible synthesis of values. This behavior, not incentivized by the evaluation structure, suggests the format's procedural logic orients participants toward integration rather than domination. Second, the unplanned bleed effect: a participant shared, unprompted during a break, that the debate scenario ``hit uncannily close to home'' because a family member had asked them that same day to provide false testimony. This moment of real-life resonance demonstrates the Inside Out format's capacity to activate personal value reflection---precisely the transformative mechanism the design aimed to support.

\subsection{Proof of Concept Assessment}

Taken together, the clues documented across both playtests provide reasonable grounds for a proof-of-concept claim. The format:

\begin{itemize}
    \item was experienced as engaging and appealing, particularly by participants underserved by traditional formats;
    \item maintained sufficient intellectual pressure through a redirection rather than removal of challenge;
    \item elicited spontaneous perspective-taking, value exploration, and contextual reasoning;
    \item generated emergent community norms oriented toward collaborative rather than adversarial discourse; and
    \item produced instances of epistemic humility, bleed effects, and personal value resonance consistent with the theoretical framework's predictions.
\end{itemize}

No single piece of evidence is conclusive. Small sample sizes, the absence of longitudinal follow-up, and the researcher's dual role as designer and observer all limit what can be inferred. However, the convergence of multiple independent clues across two separate playtest groups---each producing similar patterns of engagement, anxiety reduction, and spontaneous norm formation---provides a coherent picture that justifies continued investigation. The format warrants further empirical study with larger groups, more experienced facilitators, and longitudinal measurement of the skill and attitudinal outcomes theorized here.

\subsection{Limitations}

The original design of this study envisioned approximately four iterative playtest sessions. Two were completed within the thesis timeline. While this reduces the number of design iterations and the breadth of participant exposure, both sessions yielded substantive observational and debrief data sufficient for proof-of-concept assessment. Future work should extend the iterative cycle.

The researcher's insider position as a fifteen-year competitive debater and the format's designer is both a strength (enabling nuanced interpretation of participant behavior) and a limitation (creating potential for confirmation bias in clue selection and interpretation). Reflexivity notes were maintained throughout, and the Annex H clue list explicitly notes the evidential strength and directionality of each inference.

Finally, both playtest groups consisted of experienced debaters already familiar with competitive formats. The format's performance with novice participants---for whom the contrast with standard debate would be less salient---remains untested.
