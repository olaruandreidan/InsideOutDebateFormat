\section{RESULTS}
\label{sec:results}

The question admits two decomposed sub-questions. First: can a research method of applying transformative game design theory to this problem domain be demonstrated? Second: does that research method yield expected results? Each is addressed in turn.

\subsection{Demonstrating the Method: The Design Process as Contribution}

The first sub-question is answered by the thesis itself. The preceding chapters document a systematic progression from theoretical analysis (Sections~\ref{sec:background} and~\ref{sec:theory}), through iterative format design grounded in design theory principles (Section~\ref{sec:design}), to empirical testing and reflection. This sequence constitutes a worked example of how transformative game design theory can be operationalized in a competitive debate context. The design decisions recorded in Annexes A, B, and G---and the reasoning connecting each iteration to observed player behavior---demonstrate that such an application is coherent, achievable, and produces testable outcomes. Whether or not the resulting format succeeds transformatively, the method of application has been rendered explicit and repeatable.

\subsection{Evaluating the Results: Evidence from Playtests}

The second sub-question concerns whether the designed format shows signs of achieving its intended effects. Two playtests were conducted: the first in March 2026 with senior debaters from the Jean Monet debate club in Bucharest, and the second in April 2026 with seven veteran debaters in an informal setting. Field notes, structured debrief responses (Annex C), and post-session reflections constitute the primary data.

It is important to frame what this data can and cannot show. Given the small sample size, the absence of control conditions, and the exploratory nature of the design, the collected data cannot prove the format's effectiveness. What it can provide are \textit{clues}---observed behaviors, reported experiences, and emergent patterns---from which educated inferences about potential effectiveness may be drawn.

The analytical process through which these clues were identified, comparatively weighted, and connected to format mechanics is described in Section~\ref{sec:methods}. The complete clue taxonomy---all potential clues, observed and unobserved, with evidential sources, strength assessments, and design principle connections---is provided in Annex~I. The following analysis discusses the most significant confirmed clues across the five evaluation fields.

A methodological caveat applies particularly to clues about engagement and appeal. Participants were aware they were playtesting a format designed by the researcher present in the room, and the exclusive character of the invitation may have elevated their investment in the experience. These conditions create elevated risk of social desirability effects: positive engagement responses may be amplified; negative ones suppressed. Accordingly, behavioral and emergent clues---spontaneous norm formation, organic language adoption---are weighted more heavily than self-reported enthusiasm when assessing Field~1.

\subsubsection{Field 1: Engagement, Appeal, and Long-Term Viability}

For a debate format to sustain a community, it must be sufficiently engaging to generate voluntary, repeated participation. Several clues point in this direction, though the social desirability caveat above applies with particular force here.

Self-reported engagement was consistently positive across both playtests: participants described the format as ``surprisingly liberating,'' reported ``a lot less anxiety,'' and called the Huddle phase ``very very fun.'' These reports constitute moderate evidence only, given the playtest context. The stronger engagement signal is behavioral: in the second playtest, participants spontaneously adopted the term ``inner voices''---a term absent from the ruleset---to refer to each other during play. Organic language generation of this kind suggests genuine imaginative investment rather than performed enthusiasm, and is coded as a strong clue in Annex~I.

One negative engagement clue was observed. In the first playtest, the Rolling Out ritual---the format's closing sequence---was described as ``very robotic'' and as breaking immersion at the moment it was intended to complete the emotional arc. This is a significant finding: it indicates that immersion \textit{was} present before that point, and was disrupted by a specific design element, not by the format's premise. The ritual was redesigned in the second iteration accordingly.

The differential appeal across participant profiles is a relevant secondary finding. Women and debaters who had been less successful in traditional formats expressed consistent preference for Inside Out across both sessions; the one participant who found the format unsatisfying was a highly competitive debater who missed the ``ego-boosting'' experience of decisive victory. Whether this profile-specific response represents a limitation or a design confirmation depends on the format's intended audience.

\subsubsection{Field 2: Productive Pressure Mechanisms}

Competitive debate's transformative potential depends partly on pressure: the cognitive and social demands of the activity push participants to grow. A format without sufficient challenge risks becoming a low-stakes social exercise without developmental benefit. Clues from both playtests suggest the format redirects rather than removes intellectual pressure, though this redirection also surfaced a significant design gap.

Participants in the first playtest described ``architecting solutions'' as ``very complex and interesting strategically.'' The value-argumentation task---constructing a value priority for a specific character, defending a concrete decision, and engaging with two competing frameworks simultaneously---appears to generate genuine cognitive demand, though this self-report is at best moderate evidence. The stronger positive clue is behavioral: in the second playtest, players spontaneously produced elaborate, contextually rich speeches without being prompted, indicating sustained effort. Both clues point in the same direction and are documented in Annex~I.

The most informative finding in this field, however, is a negative clue. In the first playtest, the Subject player described not knowing ``what to say'' or how to ``play very clean,'' uncertain how to hold emotional investment and evaluative neutrality simultaneously. This role underspecification produced visible hesitation during play---a behavioral rather than self-reported signal. It directly informed the second design iteration's revised Subject speech instructions, and the confusion did not recur in the second playtest. The clue is therefore informative in two ways: it identified a specific design gap, and the gap's disappearance after correction gives some confidence that the revision was targeted correctly.

The commendation system remains an open question. Evaluators in both playtests found commendations difficult to assign consistently, pointing to a pressure-and-recognition mechanism that functions at a conceptual level but remains calibrationally immature at small, informal scale.

\subsubsection{Field 3: Growth Direction---Useful Skills and Perspectives}

A central design goal was to redirect the skills debate develops toward more transferable outcomes: persuasive communication, perspective-taking, contextual reasoning, and collaborative discourse. The clues here are mixed: strong behavioral evidence in the second playtest, moderate self-report in the first, and one significant negative clue in the first.

In the first playtest, participants articulated learning to ``think about the context of decisions,'' to be ``more human and more honest about human nature,'' and to recognize that ``sometimes decisions are personal, not universal.'' These self-reported reflections are moderate evidence: they align with the format's procedural rhetoric \parencite{bogost2007persuasive} and are on-target with design intent, but they are retrospective and unverified by pre-session baseline. Equally important is what was absent: no non-standard or unhelpful new play behaviors were rewarded, and no performative hollow emotionality was observed---negative clues that, had they manifested, would have undermined the growth-direction claim (see Annex~I).

The second playtest provides the strongest positive clues for this field. All players immediately adopted a roleplay-oriented stance---second-person address, direct eye contact with the Subject, contextual worldbuilding detail---none of which was instructed in the ruleset. This spontaneous behavior is behavioral rather than self-reported, and coded as a strong clue in Annex~I: it suggests the format's structure elicits perspective-taking without instruction, though this observation is confined to a single session and cannot yet be called reliable. The Subject player's transcript-confirmed opening speech---``I'm thinking about his situation\ldots{}I don't know if prison is really what would help him\ldots{}that's why this thing is eating at me''---demonstrates bleed from player into character before the debate had formally begun, and the player's subsequent reflection that this had led them to ``expose some nuanced biases'' constitutes a concrete instance of the memetic bleed mechanism theorized in Section~\ref{sec:theory}.

A notable negative clue was the immersion break produced by the first-iteration Rolling Out ritual, which disrupted the bleed arc at the moment it was intended to complete it. This was directly addressed in the second iteration and not observed in the second playtest.

\subsubsection{Field 4: Community Dynamics and Reduced Toxicity}

One of the design's primary goals was to reduce the hierarchical and adversarial dynamics that contribute to attrition and harm in competitive debate communities. This field presents both the most consistent positive clues and the most notable pattern of absent negative clues.

Reduced anxiety was reported consistently and independently across both playtest groups---``much much less anxiety-inducing'' than standard competitive debate, a ``feeling of salvation regardless of result,'' the sense that ``everyone distinguished himself by his own style.'' The multi-session consistency strengthens confidence in this finding beyond what a single playtest would allow. One honest caveat is offered: the format's reduced established status means less social capital is staked on performance, so some portion of the anxiety reduction may not persist in a mature Inside Out circuit. This is an empirical question for future study.

A strong behavioral clue from the second playtest is the emergent norm that ``outright attacking others' points feels wrong, disrespectful, and unnecessary.'' This was not stated in the ruleset; it arose from the format's structural logic---a procedural rhetoric outcome in the precise sense theorized by \textcite{bogost2007persuasive}. That players found collaborative framing more natural and more effective than adversarial rebuttal suggests a self-reinforcing alignment between competitive incentives and transformative design intent.

Equally informative is what was not observed. None of the three negative clues for this field---expressions of distress, arrogant or unempathetic behavior, or praise of such behavior---manifested across either playtest or in the transcripts. Given the emotional intensity of some moments in the second session (including an unplanned bleed event where the scenario resonated with a real family situation), the absence of distress is a meaningful signal that the bleed-check and debrief structure provided sufficient containment.

\subsubsection{Field 5: Epistemic Humility}

The deepest transformative aim of the format is to cultivate epistemic humility: a disposition to take multiple perspectives seriously, to recognize that different value frameworks can each be valid, and to hold one's conclusions with appropriate uncertainty. This field contains some of the strongest behavioral clues in the dataset, though all are from a single playtest session.

In the first playtest, one participant explicitly articulated: ``every team says valuable stuff and it is important more to see what is important to us.'' This moderate clue is notable for being unprompted, and for capturing precisely the attitudinal shift the format targets.

The strongest clues come from the second playtest. The spontaneous drift toward synthesis in the second round of Value Defender speeches---players converging rather than intensifying conflict, despite no rule incentivizing this---is a behavioral, transcript-confirmed, emergent indicator that the format's procedural logic orients participants toward integration rather than dominance. The Subject's opening statement, ``I'm not sure if that's the solution---that's why this thing is eating at me'' (transcript-confirmed), demonstrates genuine epistemic uncertainty from the outset of the round, produced not by instruction but by the format's demand that the Subject inhabit the dilemma rather than resolve it immediately. The unplanned bleed event---a participant sharing privately that the scenario resonated with a real-life situation they were facing that same day---is the single highest-strength clue across the entire dataset: it was spontaneous, private (not performed for the researcher), and constitutes the transformational mechanism functioning exactly as theorized in Section~\ref{sec:theory}.

No negative clues for this field---unequivocal condemnation of positions or belief-holders, or praise of such condemnation---were observed across either playtest or the transcripts. The absence is meaningful given that both motions used involved choices with genuine ethical weight.

\subsection{Proof of Concept Assessment}

Taken together, the clues documented across both playtests provide reasonable grounds for a proof-of-concept claim. The format:

\begin{itemize}
    \item was experienced as engaging and appealing, particularly by participants underserved by traditional formats;
    \item maintained sufficient intellectual pressure through a redirection rather than removal of challenge;
    \item elicited spontaneous perspective-taking, value exploration, and contextual reasoning;
    \item generated emergent community norms oriented toward collaborative rather than adversarial discourse; and
    \item produced instances of epistemic humility, bleed effects, and personal value resonance consistent with the theoretical framework's predictions.
\end{itemize}

No single piece of evidence is conclusive. Small sample sizes, the absence of longitudinal follow-up, and the researcher's dual role as designer and observer all limit what can be inferred. However, the convergence of multiple independent clues across two separate playtest groups---each producing similar patterns of engagement, anxiety reduction, and spontaneous norm formation---provides a coherent picture that justifies continued investigation. The format warrants further empirical study with larger groups, more experienced facilitators, and longitudinal measurement of the skill and attitudinal outcomes theorized here.

\subsection{Limitations}

The original design of this study envisioned approximately four iterative playtest sessions. Two were completed within the thesis timeline. While this reduces the number of design iterations and the breadth of participant exposure, both sessions yielded substantive observational and debrief data sufficient for proof-of-concept assessment. Future work should extend the iterative cycle.

The researcher's insider position as a fifteen-year competitive debater and the format's designer is both a strength (enabling nuanced interpretation of participant behavior) and a limitation (creating potential for confirmation bias in clue selection and interpretation). Reflexivity notes were maintained throughout, and the Annex~I clue list explicitly notes the evidential strength and directionality of each inference.

Finally, both playtest groups consisted of experienced debaters already familiar with competitive formats. The format's performance with novice participants---for whom the contrast with standard debate would be less salient---remains untested.
