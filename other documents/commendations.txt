INSIDE OUT DEBATE FORMAT — FULL COMMENDATION LIST
==================================================

Format notes:
- Each commendation has a NAME, a SHORT description (shown on card), and an EXTENDED body.
- Extended body has rank levels (Earned / Strong / Exceptional) and a "Do not award if" note.
- Some commendations are role-specific: [Subject only] or [Defenders only].


==================================================
CATEGORY 1: STYLE
6 commendations — delivery, presence, and rhetorical quality
==================================================

--- Believable Passion ---
Short: You were truly invested in your point, showing genuine, believable conviction and care for the value you defended.
Note: Distinct from Honest Connection, which is about personal self-disclosure. This commendation is about conviction in the argument. Distinct from Broad Range, which is about technical delivery control. This commendation is about authenticity of feeling.

Earned: Tone conveyed genuine care at key moments. Concrete signs: voice quality shifted naturally at charged points, eye contact held at key claims, word choices felt spontaneous rather than rehearsed. The debater was not merely reciting.
Strong: Conviction was felt in the substance of the speech: key claims landed harder because of the emotional weight behind them, and the room or the Subject visibly reacted (leaning in, stillness, visible attention). The emotional register clearly matched the content.
Exceptional: The delivery made the dilemma feel real and worth deeply caring about. Reserve for speeches where the affective dimension was genuinely transformative, the kind that may echo long after the debate ended.

Do not award if: passion felt theatrical without substance, or if the debater seemed to be performing emotion rather than expressing it.


--- Entertaining Discourse ---
Short: The speech was genuinely enjoyable, through wit, lightness, or a well-timed observation that made the room smile or lean in.
Note: Distinct from Poetic Appeal, which rewards literary craft. This commendation rewards the audience's enjoyment response: humor, liveliness, wit. A poetic speech can be somber and still earn Poetic Appeal; it cannot earn this.

Earned: At least one moment was distinctly enjoyable: a clever comparison, a light touch, or a well-timed observation. The speech was not dull.
Strong: Liveliness was woven into the argumentation consistently; humor or vividness served to make points land more effectively and did not merely entertain.
Exceptional: Entertainment and argument were seamlessly fused throughout, reaching the level of a sharp public talk or spoken word performance, where wit and substance were inseparable.

Do not award if: entertaining moments came at someone's expense, or were clearly decorative and disconnected from substance.


--- Broad Range ---
Short: Your delivery showed impressive stylistic range, varying tone, rhythm, tempo, and volume to serve the speech.
Note: Distinct from Believable Passion, which rewards authenticity of feeling. This commendation rewards technical delivery control and intentional variation. A debater can earn this through craft even in a measured, less emotionally charged speech.

Earned: At least two noticeably different delivery registers were used, e.g. a slow and solemn moment followed by a faster, energized passage. The speech did not feel flat or uniform.
Strong: Variation was deliberate and purposeful: shifts in pace, volume, or tone coincided with structural moments in the argument, making the speech easier to follow and more dynamic.
Exceptional: The full palette was deployed with mastery. The debater controlled the room's attention through delivery alone, knowing when to slow down, when to project, when to drop to a near-whisper. The range felt like a conscious instrument rather than an accident.
Do not award if: variation was random or distracting rather than purposeful, or if the speech was stylistically uniform throughout.


--- Honest Connection ---
Short: Something genuine about yourself entered the speech, a real disclosure rather than a rhetorical move, that created contact between the debater and the room.
Note: Distinct from Believable Passion, which is about conviction in the argument. This commendation is about self-disclosure and personal presence. A debater can argue passionately without ever revealing anything about themselves; this commendation rewards the ones who do.

Earned: At least one moment where the debater stepped out from behind the argument (an admission, a first-person reflection, or a genuine statement of personal feeling) that felt real rather than strategic.
Strong: The personal register appeared at key structural moments and gave the argument a human face. Concrete signs: eye contact moving around the room rather than fixed or absent, direct second-person address ("you know this feeling"), or a moment of deliberate pause that invited the room to sit with something.
Exceptional: The speech was uniquely theirs; you would not have heard it from anyone else. You felt the debater was genuinely present in the room with you.
Do not award if: personal moments felt scripted or confessional for effect, or if the connection was superficial without real disclosure.


--- Poetic Appeal ---
Short: Language was used with aesthetic care, where rhythm, imagery, or metaphor elevated the speech as a piece of writing, independent of its entertainment value.
Note: Distinct from Entertaining Discourse, which rewards the audience's enjoyment response. This commendation rewards literary and rhetorical craft. A precise, somber, or weighty speech can earn this; a funny speech that lacks craft cannot.

Earned: At least one passage was noticeably well-crafted: a sentence that would stand alone as good writing, a metaphor that illuminated the value, or a rhythmic construction that made a point feel inevitable.
Strong: Poetic quality sustained across multiple moments; rhetoric served the argument, making the case land harder or feel more vivid.
Exceptional: The speech had genuine literary quality; specific lines are still in your head afterward. Language itself became part of the argument, functioning as substance rather than decoration.
Do not award if: literary flourishes were borrowed or clichéd, or if aesthetic ambition exceeded communicative clarity.


--- True Embodiment ---
Short: You convincingly inhabited your role, arguing and deciding from inside it rather than about it from outside.

Earned: The role's reality guided the speech throughout. Stated leanings tracked the actual arguments made, and no decision was telegraphed from the outset.
Strong: The embodiment elevated the entire round. You could see the speaker being moved, reconsidering, weighing; the final decision felt earned and true to the character's inner life. The investment in the value was palpable enough that you could imagine them holding it beyond this room.

Do not award if: the character's perspective was abandoned in favor of what looked like generic, standard convictions.

Note: For the Subject, this means thinking, feeling, and deciding as the character they are playing. For Defenders, it means arguing from genuine conviction in the value rather than as a lawyer assigned to it: the value's internal logic should visibly drive the case, not just decorate it.

CATEGORY 2: STRATEGY
7 commendations — positioning, framing, and tactical intelligence
==================================================

--- Specific Topicality ---
Short: Arguments were sharply specific to this dilemma, this Subject, and these values, with no generic claims.

Earned: Most substantive claims were grounded in the specifics of the motion: the Subject's background, the values as stated, the actual decision at stake. The debater did not argue in the abstract when specificity was available.
Strong: Topicality was strategic. The debater drew on details others missed or ignored: specific facts from the Subject's biography, constraints embedded in the motion's exact wording, or stakes unique to this scenario. The arguments demonstrably apply to this case in ways a more generic version of the same point would not.
Exceptional: Every substantive move calibrated to this specific situation; the arguments would fall apart if you changed the Subject or dilemma, a reliable sign they were arguing this case rather than a template.
Do not award if: arguments were substantially generic and could be transplanted into a different debate without modification.

Note: Distinct from Advantageous Framing, which rewards setting up the evaluation lens. This commendation rewards the content of the arguments themselves: what you chose to argue was specific, not just how you framed the evaluative criteria.


--- Advantageous Framing ---
Short: You defined the terms of discussion in a way that benefited your position before others could.

Earned: Explicitly established a coherent evaluation framework (e.g., "the key question here is not X but Y") that was relevant to their value's claim and genuinely substantive rather than merely rhetorical.
Strong: The framing did real structural work. The other debaters had to address or rebut the framing directly in order to make their point. The Subject explicitly engaged with it, showing it had become a reference point in the room.
Exceptional: The framing was original and visibly influenced the course of the debate; much of the round can be understood in retrospect as a response to it. Award rarely.
Do not award if: the framing was stated but not developed, or simply restated the obvious rather than establishing a genuinely favorable lens.

Note: Distinct from Specific Topicality, which rewards the content of your arguments. This commendation rewards the meta-level move of setting the evaluative lens. Distinct from Helpful Guiding [Subject], which is the neutral equivalent: Guiding helps the room understand the debate fairly; Framing is partisan and serves the Defender's position.


--- Witty Solution ---
Short: You proposed a concrete, value-informed solution to the dilemma, specific enough that it could only have come from someone defending your value.

Earned: The proposed solution was specific and thought through: a concrete recommendation that addressed at least the most obvious counterarguments, and that demonstrably flowed from the value defended rather than from generic common sense.
Strong: Notably smart: the solution navigated a genuine tension between competing values, or revealed an angle others had missed. It felt like an insight that only someone genuinely reasoning from this value could have reached.
Exceptional: Elegant in the way a good solution to a hard problem is elegant: surprising, clearly defensible, and more persuasive than the alternatives precisely because of the way the value was applied.
Do not award if: the decision was generic, obvious, or could have been proposed without any particular commitment to the defended value.

Note: Distinct from Advantageous Framing, which sets up the criteria of choice. This commendation rewards the solution itself: the concrete answer to the dilemma derived from the defended value (e.g., a Defender of financial stability proposing not "prioritize stability" but "take the law school offer specifically, because it offers the fastest path to financial independence").


--- Brave Pivot ---
Short: You changed position or conceded a point honestly, and it made your case stronger.

Earned: Explicitly acknowledged an opponent's point had merit, or adjusted their position in response to what was said.
Strong: The pivot was strategic: conceding less important ground to better defend more important ground, or reframing in a way more defensible in light of the debate.
Exceptional: Changed their mind in real time under pressure and did so convincingly, arguing for why the pivot is an extension rather than an inconsistent reversal of their previous position, making the final case stronger than the initial one. Award with corresponding rarity.
Do not award if: the pivot was a disguised inconsistency, or a capitulation that weakened the case without any strategic gain.


--- Efficient Alliance --- [Defenders only]
Short: You recognized when another Defender's arguments complemented yours and built on that alignment.

Earned: Acknowledged at least one point of genuine agreement with another Defender that strengthens the case for a decision, even across different value frameworks.
Strong: Explicitly constructed an alliance: "both Team A and I agree that X, which shows the Subject that even across different value frameworks, the weight of argument points toward Y." The alliance was strategically useful.
Do not award if: the alliance was superficial without specific engagement, or used to dodge genuine tensions rather than genuinely strengthen a shared position.

Note: Distinct from Careful Positioning: forming an alliance is one possible positioning choice, but this commendation specifically rewards the act of finding and leveraging genuine agreement to jointly persuade the Subject. Careful Positioning rewards the deliberateness of any stance; Efficient Alliance rewards this specific one done well. Not applicable to the Subject, who should remain open to all arguments rather than forming alliances.


--- Careful Positioning --- [Defenders only]
Short: Your stance toward other Defenders' decisions was deliberate and chosen rather than reactive, with positive effect.

Earned: Response to other Defenders' decisions was evidently chosen rather than accidental or reactive. Concrete sign: the debater explicitly flagged the choice ("I am going to agree with Team A here because..."), or the same stance was coherently maintained across both speeches in a way that only makes sense as a deliberate strategy.
Strong: Positioning was in service of a coherent strategy; the debater demonstrated awareness of the strategic landscape alongside the argumentative one. You could reconstruct the reasoning behind every stance they took, and it formed a consistent picture.
Do not award if: the debater attacked everything indiscriminately or supported everything uncritically. The commendation rewards the conscious choice.

Note: Distinct from Efficient Alliance. Careful Positioning rewards the consciousness of the choice across all possible stances: attack, support, nuance, or alliance. A debater who deliberately attacks every other Defender can earn this; one who forms an alliance can too. Efficient Alliance rewards specifically the well-executed alliance move. Both can be earned in the same round.


--- Helpful Guiding --- [Subject only]
Short: You helped the room navigate the structure of the debate clearly, accurately, and fairly.

Earned: At least one moment of genuine meta-level navigation, e.g. "the key question before us is now X, because Y has been established and Z has been challenged." It helped the room follow more clearly.
Strong: The guiding was structurally important, identifying a genuine turning point or consolidating arguments in a way that made the final decision feel better-grounded. Accurate and fair to all arguments, including inconvenient ones.
Do not award if: the "guiding" was simply restating one's own position, or characterized the debate in a self-serving way.

Note: Distinct from Advantageous Framing [Defenders], which is partisan: it sets an evaluative lens that serves the Defender's position. Helpful Guiding is neutral: the Subject's role is to help the room understand where the debate actually stands, including arguments that cut against the Subject's apparent leaning.

CATEGORY 3: CONTENT
9 commendations — argumentation, analysis, and engagement with the debate
==================================================

--- Word Economy ---
Short: You communicated clearly and efficiently; every minute of speaking time counted.

Earned: The speech did not waste significant time; arguments were stated and developed without excessive preamble, redundant restatement, or filler.
Strong: Concision actively served clarity: complex ideas were expressed with precision and brevity; you understood them fully on first hearing, without the debater needing to repeat themselves.
Exceptional: The debater said something hard to say in few words. Specific lines had a density of meaning that rewarded attention; you notice the economy because of how much it achieves.

Do not award if: the speech was sparse because the debater ran out of things to say, rather than because of principled concision.

Note: This commendation rewards the form of expression, not argument substance. A speech can have Robust Logic and Impressive Depth but still fail on economy if it took twice as long as needed to make its points. Conversely, a speech that says little but says it crisply does not earn this: concision must serve content, not replace it.

--- Rebuttal Ingenuity ---
Short: Your rebuttal identified a non-obvious flaw in an opponent's argument: a hidden assumption, internal tension, or unconsidered consequence.

Earned: Identified a non-obvious weakness (a hidden assumption, an internal tension, or an unconsidered consequence) rather than the first available attack.
Strong: Engaged with the strongest version of the argument and demonstrated why it fails even there.
Exceptional: Changed the argumentative landscape; the rebuttal was difficult or impossible to satisfactorily respond to. Reserve accordingly.
Do not award for mere aggressiveness without analytical incisiveness; the attack must have identified something non-obvious, not just pressed hard on an obvious weakness.

Note: Distinct from Argument Ingenuity, which rewards novel constructive arguments of your own. This commendation is reactive: you found something wrong with an existing argument in a way others would not have. Distinct from Comprehensive Breadth, which rewards coverage of the full debate rather than the sharpness of any individual attack.

--- Rebuttal Prioritization ---
Short: You made deliberate, strategic choices about where to concentrate your rebuttal effort, and those choices were correct.

Earned: Concentrated force on the most important clashes, explicitly or implicitly flagging the allocation.
Strong: Prioritization revealed genuine strategic intelligence. Concrete sign: the debater's choice of what to ignore was as deliberate as what they attacked. Arguments left alone were either too weak to warrant time or too entrenched to rebut cheaply, and the choice was correct.

Do not award if the debater simply failed to address major clashes; this commendation rewards deliberate focus, not accidental omission.

Note: Distinct from Comprehensive Breadth, which rewards engaging with the full scope of the debate. These two commendations reward opposite instincts: Breadth rewards coverage, Prioritization rewards deliberate focus: choosing which clashes matter most and concentrating there, even at the cost of leaving some arguments unaddressed. Both can be earned in the same speech if a debater focuses sharply on the right clashes while still engaging meaningfully with all major ones.

--- Rebuttal Accuracy ---
Short: You represented opponents' arguments faithfully before responding, no mischaracterization, no strawmanning.

Earned: Paraphrased opponents' arguments correctly before responding; no strawmanning. Engaged with its most charitable understanding.

Strong: Engaged with specific moments in specific speeches; accuracy made the rebuttal more effective, not just fairer.

Do not award unless correct representation was genuinely non-trivial; if the argument was simple and obvious, accurate paraphrase is the baseline, not commendable.


--- Robust Logic ---
Short: Your arguments were well-constructed: valid, plausible, and without significant logical gaps.

Earned: Main arguments had clear claim-warrant-impact structure; a reasonable person could follow the chain of reasoning without supplying missing steps.
Strong: Logic held up under challenge: no internal contradictions, no analytical sleight-of-hand, no steps that required the audience to take things on faith.
Exceptional: Genuinely tight; would require a sophisticated philosophical or empirical objection to dislodge; the debater had anticipated obvious objections within the argument itself.

Do not award if: key steps required the audience to make significant logical leaps, or if the argument relied on unexamined premises.

Note: Distinct from Impressive Argumentative Depth. Logic asks whether the reasoning chain holds structurally: are the steps valid, are the premises sound, are there gaps? Depth asks how far the argument reaches: does it unpack implications, interrogate philosophical roots, go beyond the surface claim? A tight argument can be shallow; a deep argument can have structural weaknesses. Both can be earned simultaneously.

--- Impressive Argumentative Depth ---
Short: You went beyond the surface, interrogating implications, mechanisms, or philosophical roots.

Earned: At least one argument went meaningfully beyond its initial claim, explaining why the value's demand is well-founded, or what it would actually look like for the Subject to live by it.
Strong: Depth sustained throughout; consistently pushed past surface-level argumentation into territory where the debate becomes genuinely illuminating.
Exceptional: After the speech, you found yourself reconsidering something you had assumed you understood, or the argument illuminated a dimension of the value that no other speech in the round had touched. Award only when you can name the specific idea that landed with that force.

Do not award for length or confidence without depth; a long argument that merely repeats its initial claim in different words is the opposite of this commendation.

Note: Distinct from Robust Logic, which rewards structural soundness of the reasoning chain. This commendation rewards how far the argument reaches, regardless of whether the chain is airtight. Distinct from Argument Ingenuity, which rewards novelty of the argument itself. Depth rewards thorough development: you can earn this by deeply analyzing an obvious argument. Ingenuity rewards unexpectedness: you can earn it with a brief but novel point. The most impressive speeches earn both.

--- Comprehensive Breadth ---
Short: You engaged with the full scope of the debate, including inconvenient arguments.

Earned: Addressed the most important arguments from all other Defenders, not only the weakest or most convenient. Did not pretend a large portion of the debate had not happened.
Strong: Demonstrated careful listening throughout: the speech reflected the actual state of the debate rather than just the prepared position. Engaged substantively with the strongest moments of each Defender.
Exceptional: Managed to impressively capture every aspect of the debate and to synthetically yet explicitly cover all of them.

Do not award if: the debater only engaged with arguments favorable for them to rebut, or systematically ignored major clashes.

Note: Distinct from Rebuttal Ingenuity (sharpness of individual attacks) and Rebuttal Prioritization (deliberate focus on selected clashes). This commendation rewards coverage and attentiveness across the full debate. A debater can earn this without any individual rebuttal being particularly sharp or any single clash being prioritized.

--- Argument Ingenuity ---
Short: You produced an argument that was unexpected, non-obvious, and substantively illuminating.

Earned: At least one argument was notably fresh: not typically heard in this kind of debate, logically coherent, and able to withstand an obvious first objection. The novelty did not come at the expense of substance.
Strong: Ingenious in the full sense: first surprised you, then convinced you, revealing something about the value, the dilemma, or the decision that prior thinking had missed.
Exceptional: The argument changed the debate; others had to respond to it and it became a fixed point of reference. Very rare, award accordingly.
Do not award for mere unconventionality. Ingenuity requires both novelty and substance; a provocative claim without grounding is not this commendation.

Note: Distinct from Rebuttal Ingenuity, which rewards a non-obvious attack on someone else's existing argument. This commendation rewards novel constructive arguments of your own. Distinct from Impressive Argumentative Depth, which rewards thorough development. Ingenuity rewards the argument's novelty and surprise; a brief but genuinely unexpected argument earns this where a lengthy but predictable one does not.

--- Accurate Synthesis ---
Short: You gave a fair, accurate, and well-organized account of the full state of the debate.

Earned: Correctly identified the main arguments from each Defender and did not misrepresent or omit them, even where unfavorable to the decision being leaned toward.
Strong: Showed genuine comprehension of the debate's structure: an account of how arguments interacted, where they clashed, and what the real decision points were, going beyond a mere list of positions.
Exceptional: Identified the strongest and weakest versions of each Defender's case and grounded the final decision in a genuinely comprehensive account of the round, including acknowledging the best arguments against the chosen decision.
Do not award if: the Subject's account of the debate was selective, self-serving, or inaccurate, even if the decision they reached seemed reasonable.

CATEGORY 4: FAIRPLAY
3 commendations — observe team dynamics & debrief, not only speeches
==================================================

--- Teammate Support ---
Short: You actively helped your partner formulate something difficult in a way that visibly contributed to the team's performance.

Look for: visible collaboration during prep time; a second speaker building clearly on something the first struggled with; a debater helping their partner find language or structure before a speech. Award when the help was meaningful (substantive, beyond mere reassurance) and its impact on the team's performance was visible. Ask in the debrief if not fully observable.
Threshold: the help must have been substantive and have visibly affected the team's performance.


--- Teammate Solidarity ---
Short: You showed genuine support for your partner during a hard moment, without distancing yourself.

Look for: body language and verbal behavior toward a partner after a difficult moment; a second speaker who picked up where a struggling first left off without implicitly throwing them under the bus; any explicit statement of support visible to the room. The difficult moment must have been real and the solidarity genuine rather than performed. If body language was ambiguous, ask in the debrief: "was there a hard moment for your team today, and how did you respond to each other?"
Threshold: there must have been a genuinely hard moment. Award for real solidarity under real pressure.


--- Strategic Problem-Solving ---
Short: You demonstrated intelligent, adaptive problem-solving in the face of strategic challenges in the round.

Look for: a visible strategy adjustment that worked; a debater who recognized a weakness in their own case and addressed it explicitly rather than papering over it; coordinated strategic adaptation across both speeches. Award when both the problem-recognition and the solution were present and effective.
Threshold: reacting to a problem is not enough. The response must show genuine strategic intelligence that was shared by the two and discussed: both identifying and solving the problem must be visible.

CATEGORY 5: GROWTH MENTALITY
4 commendations — use the debrief; always name the specific moment in feedback
==================================================

--- Applied Feedback ---
Short: You visibly tried to apply feedback from a previous round; imperfect application still earns this.

Evidence may come from direct comparison with a prior round, or from the debater's own account in the debrief ("I tried X because you told me Y last time"). Award when the effort is genuine and the application visible. Imperfect application still earns this; what matters is the genuine attempt rather than the outcome.


--- Out of Comfort Zone ---
Short: You attempted something genuinely difficult for you and made a real go of it.

Rewards the attempt, not the result. A debater who tried genuine embodiment for the first time and partly succeeded deserves this more than one who performed comfortably within their established range. Comfort zone is individual; use the debrief to identify what was genuinely challenging for this person. Ask directly if needed.


--- Tracking ---
Short: You showed clear awareness of your own growth across debates — where you started, what you've worked on, and where you're heading.
Note: Distinct from Applied Feedback, which rewards the act of applying specific feedback in a given round. Tracking rewards the broader self-awareness of one's development arc: the debater has a conscious picture of their own progress over time, not just a response to the last thing they were told. Use the debrief to surface this; it will rarely be visible from speeches alone.

Evidence may come from the debrief: the debater can articulate what they've improved since earlier rounds, name skills they are still actively working on, and describe their own trajectory with accuracy. Ask directly: "how do you think you've grown as a debater over these rounds?" Award when the self-picture is honest, specific, and matches your own observations.
Do not award for generic self-assessment ("I think I'm getting better overall") or for accurate self-criticism without evidence of genuine reflection over time. The debater must show they have been paying attention to their own development, not just responding to prompts.


--- Resilience ---
Short: You encountered a genuinely difficult moment and came out intact, or stronger.

The difficult moment must have been real. Award for: a strong attack responded to with a composed second speech; a delivery stumble overcome mid-speech; an unexpected move handled with adaptability. Name the specific moment in feedback; this commendation must be concrete to be useful.
Do not award for simply avoiding difficulties or for a round that went smoothly throughout. Resilience requires there to have been something to be resilient about.
