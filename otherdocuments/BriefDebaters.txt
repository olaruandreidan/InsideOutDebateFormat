COMMENDATION EVALUATION GUIDE
For the Evaluator — how to identify whether a commendation was earned

General principles:
- Commendations reward genuine achievement, not effort alone. The threshold question is always:
  "Did this debater do this well enough that it meaningfully elevated the round or their case?"
- Each commendation below may be split into two or three ranks: Earned / Strong / Exceptional.
  Ranks are useful for oral feedback and for tracking badge progression across debates.
- Role notes indicate commendations especially suited to particular roles (Value Defenders, Subject),
  but most can apply to anyone.
- Where a commendation has sub-variants (e.g. Rebuttal Ingenuity / Prioritisation / Accuracy),
  identify the most fitting sub-type and name it explicitly in feedback.
- The vocabulary used below is intentionally calibrated to WS/BP judging experience: "analysis,"
  "clash," "case construction," "rebuttal," "delivery" are used in their standard debate sense.

---

STYLE

Believable Passion
What it rewards: The debater's investment in their value came through in delivery — through vocal
energy, urgency, physical presence, or emotional color — in a way that felt genuine rather than
performed.

  Earned: The debater's tone conveyed genuine care at key moments. The energy was not mechanical
  or uniform; there were peaks that matched the weight of the argument. You believed they were
  not merely reciting. (WS parallel: the style band where "words are chosen to engage the audience
  on an emotional level.")

  Strong: Passion modulated across the speech and visibly served the argument — a key claim landed
  harder, a rebuttal felt urgent, the room's attention sharpened. It was specific, not generic
  intensity.

  Exceptional: The delivery made the dilemma feel real. The affective dimension was remarkable and
  rare. Reserve this level for speeches where the passion was transformative, not merely present.

Do not award if the passion felt theatrical and disconnected from the argument's content, or if the
intensity was uniform throughout with no variation.

---

Entertaining Discourse
What it rewards: The speech was enjoyable to listen to — through wit, vivid language, an unexpected
image, a well-timed observation, or a rhetorical flourish that entertained without sacrificing
substance.

  Earned: At least one moment was distinctly enjoyable — a turn of phrase, a clever comparison, a
  light touch that made the room lean in. The speech was not dull.

  Strong: Liveliness was woven into the argumentation consistently. Humor or vividness served to
  make points land more effectively — it was not decorative.

  Exceptional: Entertainment and argument were seamlessly fused throughout. Word choice, rhythm,
  and register were consistently elevated. The speech was enjoyable from start to finish.

Do not award if any humor came at the expense of someone in the room, or if entertaining moments
were clearly disconnected from the substantive work of the speech.

---

Broad Range
What it rewards: The debater drew on a notably wide array of angles, examples, analogies, or
disciplinary perspectives — showing intellectual versatility and avoiding a one-dimensional case.

  Earned: At least two substantially different types of reasoning or evidence were deployed — e.g.,
  a philosophical argument and a concrete real-world example; a psychological and an economic lens.
  The case did not feel one-track.

  Strong: Multiple perspectives — historical, ethical, practical, interpersonal — were meaningfully
  integrated, each explained rather than name-dropped, and each strengthening rather than
  merely varying the case.

  Exceptional: Genuine intellectual range was demonstrated. Disparate domains were connected with
  insight, giving the impression of a well-read and genuinely curious mind rather than a rehearsed
  arsenal of examples.

Do not award if the breadth was superficial (three examples all making the same thin point), or if
range came at the cost of depth. The ARGO distinction between "claims with explanation" and "claims
without" applies: breadth without analysis is not this commendation.

---

Honest Connection
What it rewards: The debater let something genuine — a personal reflection, a real uncertainty, an
authentic reaction to what was said — into the speech in a way that created real contact with the
room.

  Earned: At least one moment where the debater stepped out from behind the argument and spoke as
  themselves. It could be brief — an admission, a first-person reflection, a statement of genuine
  feeling about the value — but it felt real rather than strategic.

  Strong: The personal register appeared at key structural moments and gave the argument a human
  face. The room felt addressed, not broadcast to. The connection did not compete with the
  argument; it amplified it.

  Exceptional: The speech was uniquely theirs. You would not have heard it from anyone else. The
  debater was genuinely present in the room with you. This level is rare.

Do not award if personal moments felt scripted or confessional for effect, or if "connection" was
superficial ("I personally think that...") without any real disclosure or specificity.

---

Poetic Appeal
What it rewards: The debater used language with aesthetic care — rhythm, imagery, structure,
repetition, or metaphor — in a way that elevated the speech beyond functional argumentation.

  Earned: At least one passage was noticeably well-crafted as language — a sentence that would
  stand alone as good writing, a metaphor that illuminated the value, a rhythmic construction that
  made a point feel inevitable.

  Strong: The poetic quality was sustained across multiple moments. Word choice was precise and
  elegant. The rhetoric served the argument — it made the case land harder or feel more vivid.

  Exceptional: The speech had genuine literary quality and was beautiful to listen to. Specific
  lines are still in your head afterward. The language itself became part of the argument — not
  decoration but substance. Reserve this level accordingly.

Do not award if the literary flourishes were borrowed or clichéd (stock metaphors, motivational-
poster phrasing), or if aesthetic ambition exceeded communicative clarity.

---

True Embodiment  [especially relevant for the Subject]
What it rewards: The Subject genuinely inhabited the perspective of the Subject throughout — not as
a performance, but as a real attempt to think, feel, and decide from inside that person's situation.

  Earned: The Subject's speeches were consistently aligned with the Subject's stated character and
  values. They did not import their own personal opinions. Their stated leanings tracked the actual
  arguments made in the debate. They behaved as the Subject, not as themselves in a debate
  competition.

  Strong: The Subject's decision and reasoning felt genuinely motivated from within the Subject's
  perspective. When they explained why they were persuaded, the reasoning made sense for this person
  in this situation. There was visible effort to remain open rather than predecide.

  Exceptional: The embodiment elevated the entire round. Responses to speeches felt like genuine
  reactions — you could see them being moved, reconsidering, weighing. The final decision felt
  earned and true to the Subject's inner life. This is what this format is uniquely capable of,
  and when it happens, it should be named.

Do not award if the Subject's decision showed evidence of personal opinion overriding the debate
(ignoring strong arguments for extra-debate reasons), or if their engagement with speeches was
purely intellectual with no attempt to inhabit the Subject's emotional position.

---

STRATEGY

Specific Topicality
What it rewards: Arguments were sharply and specifically relevant to this dilemma, this Subject,
and these values — not generic claims that could apply to any debate on similar themes.

  Earned: Most substantive claims were grounded in the specifics of the motion — the Subject's
  background, the values as stated, the decision as posed. The debater did not argue in the
  abstract when specificity was available.

  Strong: Topicality was strategic. The debater exploited details of the motion that others missed,
  or demonstrated that their arguments uniquely apply to this case in ways that more generic
  versions of the same point would not. (WS parallel: the ARGO standard of examples "tied to the
  claims being made" rather than presented generically.)

  Exceptional: Every substantive move was exquisitely calibrated to this specific situation. The
  arguments would fall apart if you changed the Subject or the dilemma — a reliable sign that the
  debater was arguing this case, not a template.

Do not award if arguments were substantially generic and could be transplanted into a different
debate without significant modification.

---

Advantageous Framing
What it rewards: The debater defined or reframed the terms of discussion in a way that benefited
their position — establishing what the real question is, or what the correct lens is, before others
could.

  Earned: The debater explicitly established a framework for evaluation — e.g., "the key question
  here is not X but Y" — and it was coherent and relevant to their value's claim.

  Strong: The framing shaped how subsequent arguments landed. It did real structural work, making
  the debater's arguments harder to attack and their opponents' arguments harder to sustain. It
  showed strategic foresight, not just rhetorical positioning.

  Exceptional: The framing was original and compelling and visibly influenced the course of the
  debate. Much of the round can be understood in retrospect as a response to it. Award this level
  rarely.

Do not award if the framing was stated but not developed (a rhetorical opener without structural
consequence), or if it was simply restating the obvious rather than establishing a genuinely
favorable lens.

---

Witty Solution
What it rewards: The debater proposed a decision or way of implementing their value that was clever,
non-obvious, and effectively navigated the tensions and objections in the round.

  Earned: The proposed decision was specific and had been thought through beyond the surface. Not
  just "follow value X" but a concrete recommendation that addressed at least the most obvious
  counterarguments.

  Strong: The proposed decision was notably smart — it resolved a genuine tension between competing
  values, or anticipated and defused a major objection, or revealed an angle that others had missed.
  It felt like an insight, not just a position.

  Exceptional: The decision was elegant in the way a good solution to a hard problem is elegant —
  surprising, clearly defensible, and more persuasive than the alternatives precisely because of its
  cleverness. Reserve accordingly.

Do not award if the decision was generic, obvious, or not genuinely specific to this dilemma's
constraints.

---

Brave Pivot
What it rewards: The debater changed their position, conceded a point, or shifted strategic
direction in a way that was intellectually honest and tactically well-executed — rather than
stubbornly defending a weak position.

  Earned: The debater explicitly acknowledged that an opponent's point had merit, or adjusted their
  position in response to something said. The pivot was named and not disguised as a consistent
  position they had always held.

  Strong: The pivot was strategic — they conceded the less important ground to better defend the
  more important ground, or reframed their position in a way that was more defensible in light of
  what had been said. (WS parallel: the ARGO standard of rebuttal "developed against the strongest
  version of the opponent's argument" rather than a weaker one.)

  Exceptional: The debater changed their mind in real time under pressure and did so convincingly,
  making their final position stronger than their initial one. This is genuinely difficult and should
  be awarded with corresponding rarity.

Do not award if the pivot was a disguised inconsistency (changing position without acknowledging
it), or if it was a capitulation that weakened the case without any corresponding strategic gain.

---

Efficient Alliance  [Value Defenders]
What it rewards: The debater recognized when another Value Defender's arguments complemented their
own and explicitly leveraged that alignment — building a stronger collective case for the Subject
rather than competing for the Subject's attention at all costs.

  Earned: The debater acknowledged at least one point of genuine agreement with another Defender,
  noting that it strengthens the case for a particular decision even across differing value
  frameworks.

  Strong: The debater explicitly constructed an alliance — "both Team A and I agree that X, which
  shows the Subject that even across different value frameworks, the weight of argument points
  toward Y." The alliance was strategically useful, not just collegial.

Do not award if the alliance was superficial ("I agree with some of what was said") without
specific engagement, or if it was used to dodge genuine tensions between values rather than
genuinely strengthen a shared position. Not applicable to the Subject.

---

Careful Positioning  [Value Defenders]
What it rewards: The debater managed the strategic complexity of their relationship to other
Defenders' decisions with deliberate care — neither attacking nor supporting carelessly, but
choosing their stance based on what would best serve their value's case with the Subject.

  Earned: The debater's response to other Defenders' decisions was not accidental — there was
  evidence of a considered choice about whether to attack, support, or add nuance. They were not
  simply reacting.

  Strong: The positioning was clearly in service of a coherent strategy. The debater could
  articulate (explicitly or implicitly) why their chosen stance was the one that best served their
  value's case. They demonstrated awareness of the strategic landscape, not just the argumentative
  one.

Do not award if the debater attacked everything indiscriminately or supported everything
uncritically — both suggest a lack of deliberate positioning. The commendation rewards the
conscious choice.

---

Helpful Guiding  [especially relevant for the Subject]
What it rewards: The debater explicitly helped the room navigate the structure of the debate —
summarizing where things stood, identifying key clashes, or clarifying what questions remained —
in a way that was genuinely useful rather than self-serving.

  Earned: At least one moment of genuine meta-level navigation — "the key question before us is now
  X, because Y has been established and Z has been challenged." This helped the room follow the
  debate more clearly.

  Strong: The guiding was structurally important — it identified a genuine turning point or
  consolidated arguments in a way that made the final decision feel better-grounded. The synthesis
  was accurate and fair to all the arguments made, not just the ones convenient for the speaker.

Do not award if the "guiding" was simply restating one's own position, or if it selectively
characterized the debate in a misleading or self-serving way.

---

CONTENT

Word Economy
What it rewards: The debater communicated clearly and efficiently — without unnecessary repetition,
padding, or tangential elaboration — making every minute of speaking time count.

  Earned: The speech did not waste significant time. Arguments were stated and developed without
  excessive preamble, redundant restatement, or filler. (WS parallel: no "chunks of speaking time
  unused" in the content sense, even if the time was nominally filled.)

  Strong: Concision actively served clarity. Complex ideas were expressed with precision and
  brevity — you understood them fully on first hearing, without the debater having to repeat
  themselves. The speech would not have been improved by more words.

  Exceptional: The debater said something hard to say and said it in few words. Specific lines had
  a density of meaning that rewarded attention. You notice the economy because of how much it
  achieves. This is the good-writing standard — apply it accordingly.

Do not award if the speech was sparse because the debater ran out of things to say, rather than
because of principled concision.

---

Rebuttal Ingenuity / Prioritisation / Accuracy
This commendation has three distinct sub-variants. Identify the most fitting one when awarding and
name it explicitly in feedback. The same debater may earn more than one across two speeches.

  Rebuttal Ingenuity — the rebuttal was particularly incisive, engaging with the strongest version
  of the opponent's argument rather than a convenient one.
    Earned: The rebuttal identified a non-obvious weakness — a hidden assumption, an internal
    tension, or an unconsidered consequence — rather than the first available attack.
    Strong: The rebuttal engaged with the argument at its best and demonstrated why it fails even
    there. (ARGO: "developed against the strongest versions of the opponents' arguments.")
    Exceptional: The rebuttal was elegant and decisive — it changed the argumentative landscape and
    was difficult to satisfactorily respond to. Reserve accordingly.

  Rebuttal Prioritisation — the debater made smart choices about which arguments to spend time on,
  concentrating force on the most important clashes.
    Earned: The debater spent more time on more important arguments and implicitly or explicitly
    flagged this allocation.
    Strong: The prioritization revealed genuine strategic intelligence — what they chose to leave
    alone was as telling as what they attacked. The impact was disproportionate to the time spent.

  Rebuttal Accuracy — the debater represented other Defenders' arguments faithfully, engaging with
  what was actually said rather than a convenient version of it.
    Earned: The debater paraphrased opponents' arguments correctly before responding. No
    strawmanning.
    Strong: The debater engaged with specific moments in specific speeches — "Team A said X when Y,
    and the reason this fails is Z." The accuracy made the rebuttal more effective, not just fairer.

Do not award Ingenuity for a rebuttal that was merely aggressive or confident without being
analytically incisive. Do not award Accuracy unless the correct representation was non-trivial
(i.e., the argument was complex or ambiguous enough that misrepresentation would have been easy).

---

Robust Logic
What it rewards: The debater's arguments were well-constructed — reasoning was valid, premises were
plausible, conclusions followed without significant logical gaps.

  Earned: Main arguments had clear claim-warrant-impact structure. A reasonable person could follow
  the chain of reasoning without supplying missing steps. (ARGO: "arguments mostly well explained,
  with some logical gaps.")

  Strong: The logic held up under challenge. There were no internal contradictions, no sleight-of-
  hand in the analysis, no steps that required the audience to take things on faith. (ARGO: "most
  arguments backed up by deep and compelling analysis.")

  Exceptional: The argument was genuinely tight — it would require a sophisticated philosophical or
  empirical objection to dislodge, and the debater had anticipated obvious objections within the
  argument itself.

Do not award if key steps required the audience to make significant logical leaps, or if the
argument relied on unexamined premises that a sharp opponent would have been able to exploit.

---

Impressive Argumentative Depth
What it rewards: The debater went beyond the first level of analysis — interrogating the
implications, mechanisms, exceptions, or philosophical roots of their argument in a way that made
it substantially richer.

  Earned: At least one argument went meaningfully beyond its initial claim — the debater explained
  not just what their value demands but why that demand is well-founded, or explored what it would
  actually look like for the Subject to live by it.

  Strong: Depth was sustained. The debater consistently pushed past surface-level argumentation
  into territory where the debate becomes genuinely illuminating. (ARGO: "sophisticated and nuanced
  analysis, making arguments hard to attack.")

  Exceptional: The speech changed how you think about the value or the dilemma. The depth was
  revelatory — not just rigorous but insightful. Award only for speeches where you felt genuinely
  intellectually enriched by something you had not considered before.

Do not award for length or confidence without depth — a long argument that merely repeats its
initial claim in different words is the opposite of this commendation.

---

Comprehensive Breadth
What it rewards: The debater engaged with the full scope of the debate — not just their own value
and decision, but the actual arguments made by other Defenders — showing genuine attentiveness and
intellectual generosity.

  Earned: The debater addressed the most important arguments from the other Defenders, not just the
  weakest or most convenient. They did not pretend a large portion of the debate had not happened.

  Strong: The debater demonstrated that they had listened carefully throughout and engaged
  substantively with the strongest moments of each other Defender. Their speech reflected the actual
  state of the debate, not just their prepared position. (ARGO: "sophisticated responses to most
  arguments of the opponents, including engagement with opponents' examples and evidence.")

Note: This is distinct from Rebuttal Ingenuity. It rewards coverage and attentiveness, not the
sharpness of any single attack. A debater can earn Comprehensive Breadth without any individual
rebuttal being particularly brilliant — the signal is exhaustiveness, not excellence at any one
point.

Do not award if the debater only engaged with arguments most favorable for them to rebut, or
systematically ignored the most important clashes.

---

Argument Ingenuity
What it rewards: The debater produced an argument — a line of reasoning, an example, an analogy,
a thought experiment — that was unexpected, non-obvious, and substantively illuminating.

  Earned: At least one argument in the speech was notably fresh — something not typically heard in
  this kind of debate, and which worked.

  Strong: The argument was ingenious in the full sense — it surprised and then convinced. It
  revealed something about the value, the dilemma, or the decision that prior thinking had missed.
  It made other arguments in the round look less interesting by comparison.

  Exceptional: The argument changed the debate. Other debaters or the Subject had to respond to it;
  it became a fixed point of reference for the rest of the round. Very rare — award accordingly.

Do not award for mere unconventionality. Ingenuity requires both novelty and substance — a strange
or provocative claim without grounding is not this commendation.

---

Accurate Synthesis  [especially relevant for the Subject]
What it rewards: The debater gave an accurate, fair, and well-organized account of the state of the
debate — identifying the key arguments, their interplay, and the most important clashes — rather
than selectively representing what had been said.

  Earned: The Subject's speech correctly identified the main arguments made by each Value Defender
  and did not misrepresent or omit them. The synthesis was honest, even where it was unfavorable
  to the decision they were leaning toward.

  Strong: The synthesis showed genuine comprehension of the debate's structure — not a list of
  arguments but an account of how they interacted, where they clashed, and what the real decision
  points were. (WS parallel: the ARGO reply speech standard of "identifying major points of clash
  and providing justification.")

  Exceptional: The synthesis was analytically sophisticated — the Subject identified the strongest
  and weakest versions of each Defender's case, explained what had and had not been adequately
  addressed, and grounded their decision in a genuinely comprehensive account of the round. (ARGO
  top band: "accurately identify turning points in the debate, including the strongest arguments
  and rebuttal of their opponents.")

Do not award if the Subject's account of the debate was selective, self-serving, or inaccurate —
even if the decision they ultimately reached seemed reasonable.

FAIRPLAY
[These commendations reward behavior toward other players in the room, not speech content alone.
They require the evaluator to observe team dynamics, preparation time, moments of support, and
inter-player interaction, not just the speeches. Use the debrief conversation to verify where
direct observation was limited.]

Teammate Support  ["they helped a teammate formulate something difficult"]
What it rewards: One debater actively helped their partner work through a difficult argument, frame
a position, or find the right words — in a way that noticeably contributed to the team's
performance.

How to evaluate: Look for visible collaboration during prep time; a second speaker building clearly
and successfully on something the first speaker struggled with; explicit moments of one debater
helping the other find language or structure in the pre-speech setup. Award when the help was
meaningful (not just reassurance) and when it visibly contributed to the outcome. Ask directly in
the debrief if not observable.

---

Teammate Solidarity  ["they were supportive when things were hard"]
What it rewards: A debater demonstrated genuine emotional solidarity with their partner during a
difficult moment in the round — a stumble, a weak speech, a challenging exchange — in a way that
was supportive rather than distancing.

How to evaluate: Look for body language and verbal behavior toward a partner after a difficult
moment; a second speaker who picked up where a struggling first speaker left off without implicitly
throwing them under the bus; any explicit statement of support visible to the room. The difficult
moment must have been real — not a minor hiccup — and the solidarity must have been genuine and
not merely performative.

---

Strategic Problem-Solving  ["a strategic problem-solving approach"]
What it rewards: The team (or a debater) demonstrated a notably intelligent and adaptive approach
to the strategic challenges of the round — identifying problems in their own case or in the
round's dynamics and responding with clear-headed, creative solutions.

How to evaluate: Look for moments where a team visibly adjusted their strategy and it worked; a
debater who recognized a weakness in their own position and addressed it explicitly rather than
papering over it; a partnership that showed coordinated strategic adaptation across both speeches.
Award when both the problem-recognition and the solution were clearly present and effective.

---

GROWTH MENTALITY
[These commendations require some knowledge of the debater's development trajectory, either from
a prior round in the same event or from the debater's own account in the debrief. They can be
awarded on direct observation or on the basis of the debrief conversation. Always name the specific
moment or behavior in your feedback — growth commendations must be concrete to be useful.]

Applied Feedback  ["they worked hard to apply the feedback they received"]
What it rewards: The debater clearly tried to implement feedback from a previous round, and showed
visible evidence of having done so — even if imperfectly.

How to evaluate: Evidence may come from direct comparison with a prior round, or from the
debater's own account in the debrief ("I tried to do X because you told me Y last time"). Award
when the effort is genuine and the application is visible — the debater tried, and you can see
where. Imperfect application still earns this commendation; what matters is the attempt, not the
outcome.

---

Out of Comfort Zone  ["they stepped out of their comfort zone"]
What it rewards: The debater attempted something genuinely difficult for them — a delivery style
they don't usually use, a type of argument they tend to avoid, an approach to the role that
required genuine personal stretch — and made a real attempt at it.

How to evaluate: This commendation rewards the attempt, not the result. A debater who attempted
genuine embodiment for the first time and partly succeeded deserves this more than a debater who
performed comfortably within their established range. Use the debrief to identify what was
genuinely challenging for this particular debater — "comfort zone" is individual. Ask directly if
needed.

Tracking  [especially relevant for the Subject]
What it rewards: The debater demonstrated that they were keeping accurate track of the full state
of the debate throughout — that later speeches genuinely built on everything that had been said,
not just what was convenient.

How to evaluate: In Subject speeches: check whether the arguments cited match what was actually
said, whether the Subject tracked shifts and pivots by Defenders across both rounds, and whether
the final decision genuinely built on the accumulated argument rather than a selective memory of it.
In Defender speeches: check whether rebuttals and positioning reflected an accurate and up-to-date
picture of the round's development. Award when the tracking was notably thorough — when the
debater's account of the debate would pass a fact-check.

Resilience  ["they got through difficult moments well"]
What it rewards: The debater encountered a genuinely difficult moment in the round — a strong
attack on their argument, a stumble in delivery, an unexpected move by another team — and handled
it with composure, coming out of it intact or stronger.

How to evaluate: The difficult moment must have been real, not trivial. The recovery must have
been genuine, not just survivorship. A debater who received a devastating rebuttal and came back
with a strong, composed second speech shows resilience. A debater who stumbled early in delivery
and pulled themselves together shows resilience. Identify the specific moment explicitly in your
feedback — naming it makes the commendation useful.

Do not award for simply avoiding difficulties, or for a round that went smoothly throughout.
Resilience requires there to have been something to be resilient about.
