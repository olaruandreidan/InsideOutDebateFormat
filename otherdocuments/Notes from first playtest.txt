Notes from the first playtest: 

- the players were senior debaters, between 18 and 20, from the Jean Monet debate club in Bucharest

- they had a lot of questions about the subject, how does it work as a role - it was the newest and most different part of the format
- they also had a lot of questions about whether they can do counter-argumentation, which I found a bit odd


- during prep time they had a hard time figuring out what to prep as subjects 
- they did the rituals but it felt cringe for them
- speech by speech they shed a bit more the WS mannerism of talking in the parliamentary plural and started referring to themselves as the subject -> I need to decide if the Value Defenders are also the character of the subject and should refer to themselves as such, or if they are more rather their thoughts and should refer to the subject in the second person
- we need a ritual for calling people in between speeches
- some of them picked up on how the solutions offered can be complex and realistic and nuanced
- to the subject the fact that they had to pick a solution and not necessarily a value was still something confusing - must clarify in the rules + maybe we can do some example mini-speeches

- the format demands a different form of more philosophical argumentation that is uncommon in WS and it was hard for them to find more things to say - values need to be argued for or against or ranked in absence of practicalities, for their own sake 

- they felt the need for the subject to be an even more fleshed out character, for it to be super clear who they are so that they can formulate arguments on what values are more or less important -> this does reveal how people generally formulate narratives about their own identity and decide their identity, looking at the coherence of past actions in an attempt to become a narratively coherent individual

- they felt that the whole format waas surprisingly liberating. That they felt a lot a lot less anxiety; that architecting solutions was actually very complex and interesting strategically; that every part of the context was something that could become argumentative material

- one of them (the type that usually wins a lot at competititons) felt that the cooperative nature of the format takes away some of the thrill of colegial competitive jabbing, the adrenaline of winning, that it overall felt a lot more anticlimatic -> it might be that this format of debate will, instead of replacing the other type, be an alternative for those that seek a different kind of experience


- they did recognize that the debate plays differently: that explaining long consequential mechanistic chains is less needed as there is no need to think of large scale consequences (that is not true, it could still be true if the dillema was of a person with power over others); that the power to formulate solutions made it easier for them to stay topical and made the whole debate less vague; that it was less about proving large scale 3rd order impacts and more about real dillemas

- they felt that they spoke slower, but more monotonously and less theatrical -> although the theatricalness that comes with a parliamentary simulation was slightly lowered, I felt that they did not speak more monotonously, especially the team that did understand that the subject can be emotionally moved as well. In fact, some of them said that were they to debate again, they would try to be more emotionally immersed, and they would do that instead of trying to be very "sweaty" towards winning

- they did feel that this particular format would teach them in time 
to be more relaxed, to enjoy playing, to think about the context of decisions, to be more human and more honest about human nature; to look at the identity of decision making / to accept that "every team says valuable stuff and it is important more rather to see what is important to us" (which I guess might also mean that sometimes decisions are not universal, they are personal and arguing that someone is morally wrong when deciding or believing something is a bit absurd). 

- some of them felt it is a format more suitable for beginners -> I agree but I also believe that were I to give them a more complex motion they could recognize how it could be played at a higher skill level as well

- many ppl requested POIs 

some thoughts that I had on the format: 
- the subject team should get some thinking time before their final speech (2 min)

- commendations should be offered in secret, and then team members can approach the evaluator after the debate in private

- we should add other forms of interactions in between them -> direct questions from the subject; the huddle; maybe even POIs. In the next one I would add POIs and the huddle, and remove the interstitial speech of the subject. The huddle is a 10 minute interval in which the team of the subject can ask direct questions to the three teams, give them the floor to answer and take away their right to speak at any point, essentially controlling the conversation towards trying to resolve argumentative tensions and mayhaps force some arguments that need to interact to interact.

- the subject team should be given something to do during prep time -> I would have them prep a first speech, in which they explain their dilemma, and why is it so hard. Give real gravitas to the dilemma, prove to everyone that the dilemma is truly important and truly hard, and that all values are relevant to it. 

- the context should include a longer list of things that the subject believes about themselves and also of decisions that they took in the past

- instruction for the speeches of the subject need to also be a bit more explicit
