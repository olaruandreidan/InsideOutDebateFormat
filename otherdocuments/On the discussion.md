Some insights that I had from this: 

- if alibi to play is to have fun, then it must all sound cool, and so elements that are too self-serious will be ridiculed. If alibi to play is transformation, then elements that are off-genre again will be rejected 

- on the topic of biases. Mayhaps the actual reason for value convergence is the fact that debate has us roleplay as judges as the "average and unbias" person. Everyone roleplays that all the time. This leads us all to try to converge to roleplaying the same person, an alignment slowly building up. However, the words "average" and "unbias" are in themselves ideologically charged. People want to perceive of themselves as unbias and as having the same values as most people. This means that they will sanction those that portray this role differently. In time this will lead to a pressure to homogenise. This in turn will teach everyone that being persuasive equals whatever works in persuading this person. This is the most "average" and often working form of persuasion. Everything else is a minority. 

By having people roleplay a different person you deal with this most. And also having 3 teams, the focus on values, all of these add too. And the S1 speech reveals these biases of interpretation of the character that the Subject plays. It gives the Subject worldbuilding capabilities which is great, as it allows it to justify (so to have alibi) roleplaying different from a different Subject in a different debate - nobody is wrong, they just chose to steered for what they view as interesting.

In that sense, switch side debating does naturally anyway bring about humility. We just need to defeat the bleed-in that comes from social pressure when RP-ing something vague and not having the alibi to do it your way.

- engaging with the theory of steering is interesting as I believe it explains how you can ask the Subject to both RP but also to look analytically at what happened in the debate

- engaging with the theory of Nguyen from Art of Agency, it is in a way another expression of Bogost's point 

- the process of design itself was interesting. In a way although the playtests did form very important proofs of how the game works, discussing the issues of debate and how they could be solved was perhaps the tool I used most, I had just so many conversations and they were all so useful and too often the process of design (except in a game design studio) is so lonely and it makes no sense to be lonely, but even when done in a group the best format is still to have only one designer and the other ones to be advisors to not have decision making capabilities cuz then you get to having to do design by comittee

- another interesting point about the design system is that it was a lot about retroactively trying to fit some sort of theoretical justifications to my design. Although the thesis itself to some extent behaves as if the design emerged from the theoretical analysis, that is just academic smoke and mirrors. I designed things as it felt right and then I tried to justify it, imperfectly. In a sense the fact that things clicked theoretically shows my skill as a debater and a wordsmith and not at all the validity of this process - a decent design was achieved in spite of the absurdity of this academic system and not because of it

- it is interesting to observe that this type of games need to be addressed at teenagers (at most also early 20s students) especially, nobody else has time to do this for long periods of time. That is why it was so hard to organize playtests

- many of the issues in debate have nothing to do with the formal rules, but with the way in which the meta evolves because of structural reasons. This was revealed to me when discussing the 2nd playtest with one of the participants .For example speedtalking is much more acceptable nowadays than it was 10 or 20 years ago. That is because of a weird reason: the BP circuit became very big and as a consequence the uni teachers that originally created stopped being at all involved. This meant that it became one in which the interpretation and small evolution of the rules ended up being led by those in positions of social power. They tended to prioritise logical sophistication, as it was the hardest skill to master and the one that allowed them to retain social power. Over the decades this led to the growth of speedtalking. At a certain point a merge of the two competitive circuits, the WS and the BP one happened. In the WS circuit the rules interpretation and evolution was for decades handled by teachers very concerned with the educational value of the format. As these people grew old, many of the judges ended up being former highschool debaters, now at uni and deeply involved in the BP circuit and adopting the opinion that what happens there must be how good debating looks like, applying pressure for the format to change in WS as well. 

This means that building a new format and trying to make it work within an already existing community might lead it to suffer from the exact same issues, irrespective of its ruleset. The governance structure that defines how rules are interpreted, how they evolve, how they are applied becomes more important, and it is something that I have not designed at all

- a lot of the growth potential comes down to the training that evaluators receive, which has not been designed at all, to guidelines on how motions are to be constructed (again not designed at all), on guidelines about how commendations are to be constructed and offered and displayed, throughout the year (again not designed) 

- in fact my design has 5 components and honestly all 5 of them can be independently integrated into other debate formats: the commendation system, that could even exist across multiple formats; dividing the judge in two, one that is inside the debate and is also being evaluated but that decides the outcome of the conversation and one that is outside the debate - again this can also be applied in a different format; having three teams; having a judge that roleplays a particular person; having the teams have prescribed to them values but not specific solutions, and being allowed creative freedom to construct and to even adjust that solution
