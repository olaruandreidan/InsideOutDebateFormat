One relevant note is that the fact that people know they are part of a playtest with the designer present does make it then socially desirable for them to display enthusiasm, in order to please the designer. Furthermore, the exclusive nature of the experience may actually elevate their level of engagement and entertainment. This not to say that playtests do not yield good data: they do, especially related to pain points in the design and in showcasing strategies that may evolve. It is however not a very good indicator especially of potential popularity, all clues related to that in particular ought to be taken with a palm of salt. 

Type of clues I am looking for: 

1. Clues that it was fun/engaging and that it made people want to play this again
- people expressing it without prompting
- people expressing high and authentic levels of enthusiasm when prompted
- people talking passionately about how the experience was for them especially amongst themselves
- people laughing, having fun or being highly passionate or focused during it
- people unpromptedly recognising the benefits of playing it and thus constructing for themselves an alibi to play

1* Clues that people got frustrated, got bored, didn't really like it: 
- people being taciturn afterwards
- people being critical afterwards
- people losing focus during the debate itself
- people saying that they would more rather play regular debate and that this form could get boring or is less engaging
- people feeling that something is cringe

2. Clues that it did push people to try hard: 
- people displaying high levels of engagement 
- people actually performing at a high level 
- people talking a lot afterwards after the debate about what they did
- people displaying drive to do better were they to play this in the future

2* Clues that people didn't feel like performing
- people being apathetic 
- people only talking about how they could do better when prompted
- people being confused what they should do, how to do better

3. Clues that it makes people learn how to do stuff that is useful: 
- people displaying play patterns different from those in regular debates and which showcase and build skills that would transfer well in real life scenarios
- people continuing to display positive patterns of play that are standard to debate
- good patterns of play being rewarded and appraised during Subject speech, during Oral Critique and during debrief


3* Clues that it teaches some weird behaviours
- people being rewarded still by weird patterns of play
- people displaying new and weird patterns of play

4. Clues that it could lead to a wholesome vibe
- people expressing (ideally unprompted) feeling safer, less anxious to perform
- people displaying brave patterns of play, going out of their comfort zone with enthusiasm, and then being rewarded / praised for it 
- people during debrief displaying emancipatory bleed


4* Clues that it could lead to a toxic and competitive, un-empathetic vibe
- people being distressed during or after it
- people engaging in and (even worse) being praised for arrogance or other forms of behaviour that may cause bad-feels
- the parts of the design 


5.Clues that it could lead people to accept multilateral thinking
- people, being unprompted, becoming aware that it could 
- people displaying afterwards some degree of understanding of the personal nature of values

5* Clues that it could lead to people thinking they know everything and that those that don't must be idiots or ideological enemies
- people displaying and (even worse) being praised for unequivocal condemnation of certain beliefs or of people displaying such beliefs
